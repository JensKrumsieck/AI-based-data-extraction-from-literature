## Quantifying uncertainty in crop model predictions due to the uncertainty in the observations used for calibration

### Roberto Confalonieri a,∗, Simone Bregaglio b, Marco Acutis b

a Università degli Studi di Milano, DEMM, Cassandra Lab, via Celoria 2, 20133 Milan, Italy

b Università degli Studi di Milano, DISAA, Cassandra Lab, via Celoria 2, 20133 Milan, Italy

#### a r t i c l e i n f o

Article history: Received 11 December 2015 Received in revised form 20 February 2016 Accepted 22 February 2016 Available online 9 March 2016

Keywords: Calibration Latin hypercube sampling Model uncertainty Simplex optimization method WARM

#### a b s t r a c t

Despite modellers are paying increasing attention to analyse and manage the different sources of uncertainty affecting model predictions, the impact of the uncertainty in the observations used for calibration has been ignored. This study proposes a methodology for its quantification and provides an illustrative case study with data collected in two field experiments where rice was grown under flooded conditions in northern Italy in 2002 and 2004. Latin hypercube sampling was used to generate virtual series of observations from the mean and standard deviation of aboveground biomass values collected during the season in the two experiments. Each of the generated series was then used to calibrate the parameters maximum radiation use efficiency and optimum temperature for growth of the WARM model by means of the simplex optimization algorithm. The analysis of the distribution of key outputs (aboveground and panicle biomass at harvest) and of agreement metrics revealed that the impact of uncertainty in the observations used for calibration (explored here running calibration experiments for each of the generated series) can be large. The difference between maximum and minimum aboveground biomass at maturity was 2.79 t ha−1 and 3.78 t ha−1 for the datasets collected in 2004 and 2002, respectively. Corresponding values for panicle biomass were 0.97 t ha−1 and 2.36 t ha−1. In all cases, model outputs were normally distributed. Large differences were achieved also in the values of the agreement metrics, with RRMSE ranging from 13.64% to 36.22% and from 8.04% to 29.97% for the 2004 and 2002 datasets. The methodology proposed – although applicable to a variety of models and domains – deals only with the uncertainty due to random errors, which could derive, e.g. from non-representative sampling or from the repeatability of the method used to determine the variable of interest. Other sources of uncertainty, like those involved with systematic errors, need to be addressed in further studies. This study highlighted the need for conceptual and mathematical frameworks where the different sources of uncertainty affecting model predictions can be analysed in an integrated way.

#### **1. Introduction**

The famous statement from [Golomb](#page-4-0) [(1971)](#page-4-0) "Confusing models with reality is like to go to a restaurant and eating the menu" depicts efficaciously the way mathematical modellers should look at the products oftheir researches. Despite their usefulness, indeed, models are affected by different sources of uncertainty that should be carefully analysed to identify priorities for model improvement and to provide users with quantitative information on output reliability for specific objectives and under specific conditions of applications.

The crop modelling community has paid increasing attention to issues related to uncertainty over the last decade (e.g. [Roux](#page-5-0) et [al.,](#page-5-0) [2014).](#page-5-0) [Monod](#page-5-0) et [al.(2006)id](#page-5-0)entified differenttypes of factors within uncertainty studies involving crop models: sets of alternative model structures, parameters, and input variables (the latter including static ones needed for model initialization and dynamic drivers such as daily weather series). Uncertainty due to alternative model structures ([Sándor](#page-5-0) et [al.,](#page-5-0) [2016)](#page-5-0) recently led scientists to create international networks with the aim of increasing the reliability of model predictions by means of multi-model ensembles, e.g. AgMIP [(Rosenzweig](#page-5-0) et [al.,](#page-5-0) [2013)](#page-5-0) and MACSUR [(Rötter](#page-5-0) et [al.,](#page-5-0) [2013).](#page-5-0) Within theAgMIPRice Teamactivities, a taxonomy-based approach was recently used to explicitly analyse the relationship between model structure and model outputs [(Confalonieri](#page-4-0) et [al.,](#page-4-0) [2015).](#page-4-0) The method allowed to conclude that differences in structure often result in similar predictions and that similar structures can lead to large differences in model outputs. The authors hypothesized that subjectivity during calibration may hide expected relationships

between model structure and behaviour. Parameter uncertainty has been widely studied by crop modellers, who applied a variety of techniques to analyse its impact on model predictions (e.g. [Wang](#page-5-0) et [al.,](#page-5-0) [2005;](#page-5-0) [Confalonieri](#page-5-0) et [al.,](#page-5-0) [2010),](#page-5-0) or used Bayesian techniques to derive parameter values and related uncertainty (e.g. [Makowski](#page-4-0) et [al.,](#page-4-0) [2002;Wallach](#page-4-0) et [al.,](#page-4-0) [2012;](#page-4-0) [Dzotsi](#page-4-0) et [al.,](#page-4-0) [2015).](#page-4-0) Sensitivity and uncertainty analysis techniques were used to quantify the impact on model outputs of uncertain static inputs, like those involved with soil properties [(Varella](#page-5-0) et [al.,](#page-5-0) [2012),](#page-5-0) management practices ([Waha](#page-5-0) et [al.,](#page-5-0) [2015)](#page-5-0) and initialization variables [(Roux](#page-5-0) et [al.,](#page-5-0) [2014).](#page-5-0) Uncertainty in weather variables can be estimated either using multi-year series of measured or generated weather variables [(Monod](#page-5-0) et [al.,](#page-5-0) [2006),](#page-5-0) or with methods combining standard sensitivity analysis techniques and weather generators [(Confalonieri,](#page-4-0) [2012).](#page-4-0)

Is this enough? The uncertainty due to calibration per se is probably something on which we could invest more. On the one hand, this source of uncertainty can be due to the user subjectivity in selecting the calibration method among those available, ranging from simple trial-and-error procedures to complex optimization algorithms. Moreover, besides the specific calibration method, subjectivity can also affect calibration results for how the user selects the parameters to calibrate and defines their ranges and distributions, for the presence of interactions among parameters and, in case of optimization algorithms, for the way they are parameterized, e.g. maximum number of iterations, convergence criteria ([Wallach](#page-5-0) et [al.,](#page-5-0) [2001).](#page-5-0) On the other hand, calibration can be affected by the uncertainty in the observations used to estimate the objective function [(Roux](#page-5-0) et [al.,](#page-5-0) [2014).](#page-5-0) Although criteria for evaluating the quality of experimental datasets were recently proposed (e.g. [Kersebaum](#page-4-0) et [al.,](#page-4-0) [2015),](#page-4-0) they are – to a large extent – based on rules derived from expert opinion. [Harmel](#page-4-0) [and](#page-4-0) [Smith](#page-4-0) [(2007)](#page-4-0) incorporated measurement uncertainty into goodness-offit metrics used during the calibration of hydrological models. This approach, although valuable for calibration purposes, does not allow an explicit quantification of the impact of the uncertainty in measurements on model predictions. According to the authors, indeed, procedures for explicitly quantifying the portion of uncertainty in model outputs due to the uncertainty in the observations used for calibration are not available. The reasons why modellers investigated all other sources of uncertainty while forgetting this one are likely implicit in the sentence from the famous astronomer [Shapley](#page-5-0) [(1969):](#page-5-0) "No one trusts a model except the man who wrote it; everyone trusts an observation except the man who made it".

Uncertainty due to the data used for model calibration can derive from possible inconsistencies between the temporal resolution of observations and the scale of simulation [(Zhao](#page-5-0) et [al.,](#page-5-0) [2015).](#page-5-0) The same consideration is valid – when large area simulations are run – for the spatial resolution of measurements, and this is especially true in the case of extreme weather events [(Webber](#page-5-0) et [al.,](#page-5-0) [2016),](#page-5-0) because of their complex and fine spatial granularity. Other sources of uncertainty occur when observations are not representative of the modelled processes, or when they are affected by systematic errors because, e.g. of miscalibrated instruments.

But, even when measured data are coherent with the spatial and temporal scales of the simulation and with the way knowledge is formalized in the model, we should remember that observations are not true by definition: e.g. trueness and precision of field methods are mostly unknown [(Confalonieri](#page-4-0) et [al.,](#page-4-0) [2014),](#page-4-0) and sample size is rarely estimated before collecting samples or performing estimates. Moreover, samples can be contaminated or inadequately stored before being analysed. Even when methods are correctly applied and replicated measurements are performed, the "true value" for an observation is different from the mean of replicates. The point is: how different? Which is the impact on calibration? (and, in turn, on model output?)

The aim of this study was to propose a methodology to quantify the uncertainty in model output due to the uncertainty in the observations used for the calibration of model parameters. The methodology, developed – and thus appropriate – only for random errors, is illustrated by means of a simulation experiment based on data collected in northern Italy in 2002 and 2004 in two rice fields, and on the extensive use of an automatic optimization algorithm to avoid including in the study the uncertainty due to the user subjectivity during calibration.

#### **2. Materials and methods**

#### 2.1. A methodology for quantifying the uncertainty in model predictions due to random errors in observations

Given a series of observations (e.g. state or rate variables measured in different moments), the methodology is based on the generation of a number of possible (virtual) series of observations from the means and standard deviations of the original data. The rationale for generating the series is that – given the meaning of averaging replicated measurements – each of the generated series couldhave beencollected because ofthe uncertainty (repeatability) ofthe method for determining the variable and ofthe heterogeneity among sampled entities. Under the assumption of normality of distributions, the virtual series of observations can be generated using the Latin hypercube sampling (LHS), a type of stratified Monte Carlo sampling technique [(McKay](#page-5-0) et [al.,](#page-5-0) [1979;](#page-5-0) [Iman](#page-5-0) et [al.,](#page-5-0) [1981).](#page-5-0) Given the efficient stratification properties of LHS, a sample size of 10n can be used [(Helton](#page-4-0) [and](#page-4-0) [Davis,](#page-4-0) [2003;](#page-4-0) [Jia](#page-4-0) et [al.,](#page-4-0) [2007),](#page-4-0) with n being the number of observations available in the dataset.

The availability of the generated series allows to perform the calibration by properly accounting for the uncertainty in measured data used for estimating the objective function. For each series of generated observations, indeed, model parameters can be automatically calibrated using an optimization algorithm. To test our methodology, we used a multi start point, bounded (for parameter ranges) version of the downhill simplex method [(Nelder](#page-5-0) [and](#page-5-0) [Mead,](#page-5-0) [1965).](#page-5-0) This optimization algorithm is based on a geometrical concept, named simplex, consisting of N + 1 vertices interconnected by line segments and polygon faces in an N-dimensional parameter hyperspace. The simplex moves through this space according to three basic rules: reflection, contraction, and expansion. Although a variety of optimization algorithms not using derivatives is available in the literature (e.g. [Kirkpatrick](#page-4-0) et [al.,](#page-4-0) [1983;](#page-4-0) [Glover,](#page-4-0) [1986;](#page-4-0) [Dorigo,](#page-4-0) [1992;](#page-4-0) [Matsumoto](#page-4-0) et [al.,](#page-4-0) [2002),](#page-4-0) we used the simplex because of its good performance with respect to the ease of implementation ([Press](#page-5-0) et [al.,](#page-5-0) [2007).](#page-5-0) However, the methodology proposed here can be applied with whatever optimization algorithm.

The distribution of model outputs obtained for the array of calibrations (one for each generated series) allows to analyse the propagation of uncertainty from observations to model predictions.

#### 2.2. An illustrative case study

Datasets used for this study refer to two field experiments carried out in northern Italy in Opera (45◦22 N, 9◦12 E) in 2004 and in Vignate (45◦29 N, 9◦22 E) in 2002; datasets are described in detail by [Confalonieri](#page-4-0) et [al.](#page-4-0) [(2009).](#page-4-0) Two Indica-type cultivars (Gladio in Opera and Sillaro in Vignate) were scatter seeded and grown under flooded conditions in a completely randomized block design with four and three replicates in Opera and Vignate, respectively. Experimental factors where nitrogen (N) fertilization and cultivar in Opera, N fertilization in Vignate. Experimental plots were 35 m2 (7 m × 5 m) in Opera and 30 m2 (10 m × 3 m) in Vignate. For both experiments, data on sowing and sampling dates are shown in Table 1, as well as mean and standard deviation of measured aboveground biomass values (AGB, t ha−1).

Data shown in Table 1 were used to generate virtual series of 12 observations for Opera-2004 and of six observations for Vignate-2002. The generated series of observations were used to calibrated the parameters of the rice model WARM (e.g. [Confalonieri](#page-4-0) et [al.,](#page-4-0) [2009,](#page-4-0) [2010),](#page-4-0) used by the European Commission's Joint Research Centre for rice monitoring and yield forecasting. WARM simulates daily biomass accumulation using a net photosynthesis approach, with radiation use efficiency modulated by temperature, senescence, saturation of enzymatic chains, and atmospheric CO2 concentration. Partitioning to storage organs is reproduced by means of a set of beta and quadratic functions, driven by development stage and by the biomass partitioned to leaves at emergence. Green leaf area increase (LAI) is derived from leaf biomass and a dynamic specific leaf area, whereas senescence is simulated as a function of thermal time accumulated by each daily-emitted LAI unit. A micrometeorological module allows using temperature at the meristematic apex for phenological development and cold- and heat-induced spikelet sterility, whereas mid-canopy temperature is used for biomass accumulation and leaf blast infection. Model algorithms are fully described in the seminal literature and in the model documentation [(www.cassandralab.com/applications/2)](http://www.cassandralab.com/applications/2). In this study, the daily time step version of WARM was used. Since the two datasets were collected under unlimiting conditions for water and nutrients, and fields were properly kept weed-, pest- and disease free, simulations were carried out for potential conditions, with driving variables represented by maximum and minimum air daily temperature (◦C) and global solar radiation (MJ m−2 d−1).

Previous studies allowed identifying maximum radiation use efficiency (RUE, g MJ−1) and optimum mean daily temperature for growth (Topt, ◦C) as the parameters with the largest influence on AGB accumulation under the conditions explored in this study [(Confalonieri](#page-4-0) et [al.,](#page-4-0) [2010).](#page-4-0) The range of values allowed for RUE and Topt during calibrations were 2.6–3.2 g MJ−1 and 26–30 ◦C, respectively [(Confalonieri](#page-4-0) et [al.,](#page-4-0) [2010),](#page-4-0) whereas the starting guess to initialize the simplex was 2.9 g MJ−1 and 28 ◦C for RUE and Topt [(Confalonieri](#page-4-0) et [al.,](#page-4-0) [2009).](#page-4-0) The other parameters were set to the model default for European Indica-type cultivars ([Confalonieri](#page-4-0) et [al.,](#page-4-0) [2009).](#page-4-0) The choice of limiting to a minimum the number of calibrated parameters is coherent with what is often suggested in modelling studies (e.g. [Refsgaard,](#page-5-0) [1997;](#page-5-0) [Wallach](#page-5-0) et [al.,](#page-5-0) [2001).](#page-5-0) However, there are no theoretical reasons that prevent applying the proposed procedure to complex systems where several parameters needs to be simultaneously calibrated. The objective function that was minimized was the relative root mean square error (RRMSE, %, from 0 (optimum) to +∞) between measured and observed AGB values.

#### **3. Results and discussion**

The parameter sets derived from the array of calibrations performed using the populations of observations generated by the LHS led to the frequency distributions shown in [Fig.](#page-3-0) 1 for AGB and panicle biomass (PB, t ha−1) at maturity, and for RRMSE. For both the Opera-2004 (left column in [Fig.](#page-3-0) 1) and Vignate-2002 (right column) datasets, model outputs were characterized by a large variability, which was larger for the Vignate-2002 datasets ([Fig.](#page-3-0) 1b for AGB and d for PB) than for Opera-2004 [(Fig.](#page-3-0) 1a for AGB and c for PB). The difference between maximum and minimum AGB values was 2.79 t ha−1 for Opera-2004 (standard deviation (s) = 0.52 t ha−1) and 3.78 t ha−1 for Vignate-2002 (s = 0.77 t ha−1). Corresponding values for PB were 0.97 t ha−1 (s = 0.19 t ha−1) and 2.36 t ha−1 (s = 0.27 t ha−1). In all cases, normal distributions were obtained for both AGB and PB according to the [Shapiro](#page-5-0) [and](#page-5-0) [Wilk](#page-5-0) [(1965)](#page-5-0) test (p > 0.05). The lower variability of simulated values observed for the Opera-2004 dataset was likely due to the higher number of observations available (12 against the six available for Vignate-2002). Indeed, if we assume that measurement errors are normally distributed (i.e. the measuring method is not affected by a systematic bias),the larger the number of observations,the larger the balance between measurements overestimating and underestimating the population mean. This means that – with very few observations – the possibility of over- or underestimating most of them is higher, and this could increase the uncertainty during calibration. This hypothesis could be supported also by the coefficients of variation of the observations from the two datasets (Table 1): in most cases they were larger for Opera-2004 (the one where the variability in simulated output was lower). This could lead to hypothesize that – when data are collected with the specific aim of calibrating model parameters – it could be better to perform many uncertain measurements during the growing season rather than few very accurate measurements [(Hunt,](#page-4-0) [1990).](#page-4-0) This consideration, if confirmed, would affect the way the available (limited) resources can be invested during field campaigns where data are collected for calibration purposes.

[Fig.](#page-3-0) 1e and f show the frequency distributions for the values of RRMSE obtained at the end of the calibrations performed using the generated series of observations. Even in this case, values were quite spread, ranging from 13.64% to 36.22% for Opera-2004 and from 8.04% to 29.97% for Vignate-2002. Despite their variability, RRMSE values were coherent with values achieved in other studies on rice modelling (e.g. [Bouman](#page-4-0) [and](#page-4-0) [van](#page-4-0) [Laar,](#page-4-0) [2006;](#page-4-0) [Timsina](#page-4-0) [and](#page-4-0) [Humphreys,](#page-4-0) [2006),](#page-4-0) and they rarely exceeded the 30% threshold, often intended as the one that, if surpassed, indicates poor model performance (e.g. [Jamieson](#page-4-0) et [al.,](#page-4-0) [1991).](#page-4-0) Although most of the series generated by the LHS led to RRMSE values poorer than those obtained using the series corresponding to the mean of measurement replicates (Table 1), some of them allowed the simplex achieving lower (better) values for the metric.

#### **4. Conclusions**

We proposed a procedure to quantify the impact of uncertainty in the observations used for calibrating model parameters on the uncertainty in model outputs. The methodology is valid only for the uncertainty related to random errors: further studies are needed to develop approaches for analysing the impact of other sources of uncertainty, like those involved with the representativeness of spatiallydistributedobservations incase oflarge area applications or to systematic errors. The case study – involving a crop model – should be considered as purely illustrative. Indeed, it aimed only at providing modellers with a simple example of how the methodology can be applied, and at demonstrating that the uncertainty in observations should be part of studies targeting the quantification of model uncertainty exactly like other sources of uncertainty. As underlined by [Monod](#page-5-0) et [al.](#page-5-0) [(2006),](#page-5-0) sources of uncertainty modellers are dealing with should thus include model structure, parameters and inputs. We would add to these sources of uncertainty the calibrationtechniques,theuser subjectivityduring calibration(even when optimization algorithms are used, since they need to be parameterized), and – as discussed in this study – the observations used to calibrate model parameters. Moreover, we think modellers should try to develop conceptual and mathematical frameworks where the different sources of uncertainty affecting model predictions are considered simultaneously, i.e. in an integrated way. Indeed, it is reasonable to hypothesize that studies targeting one or two sources of uncertainties while treating the others as not uncertain (what we currently do) could lead to distortions in our findings.

Last but not least, the methodology proposed could be useful to quantitatively assess the suitability of datasets for the purpose of calibrating model parameters. Indeed, the methodology allows estimating the amount of variability in model predictions (e.g. the coefficient of variation in simulated yields) due to the uncertainty in observations. Thus, datasets could be evaluated according to the fact that the coefficient of variation in model predictions they are responsible for is below a certain threshold.

For readers interested in applying the proposed procedure, software codes implementing the simplex and other optimization methods are available for different languages and environments (e.g. Mathews and Fink, 2004; Press et al., 2007), whereas utilities implementing Latin hypercube sampling are available in most statistical packages (e.g. R, MATLAB). Model-independent software packages implementing both the Latin hypercube sampling and simplex methods are also available (Doherty, 2015).
