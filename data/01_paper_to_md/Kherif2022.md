#### ABSTRACT

Soil-crop models are widely used as valuable tools to assess the combined effects of cropping practices, soil management and climate on the agro-environmental indicators. They provide a wide range of predictive information that are useful to design and evaluate innovative cropping systems. However, intercropping modeling is still under development, especially for grain legumes-based intercropping system. We performed here the first calibration of the STICS (v 9.2) model on chickpea grown under contrasting nitrogen (N) levels during two copping seasons (2018/2019 and 2019/2020). This calibration allowed us to simulate a wide range of agronomic scenarios (climate, N-fertilization and cropping system) to optimize intercrops (durum wheat-chickpea) management. 37 parameters were estimated by using a sequential optimization method. Our results showed that STICS performs well in predicting Leaf Area Index (LAI), above ground biomass (AGB) and N uptake (AGPN) for both intercropped and sole cropped species, with satisfactory model efficiency (EF ranged from 0.62 to 0.93). In addition, grain yield was correctly predicted by the model with small error (NRMSE≤13%) especially for wheat crop (EF≥0.50), while it was less correctly predicted for chickpea crop (EF≤0.24 and NRMSE≤21%). STICS predicted well root depth under the conditions of our field study (EF ≥ 0.65 and NRMSE ≤ 37%). For soil outputs variables, the model simulated adequately soil water content with a satisfactory model efficiency (EF ≥ 0.65) and low relative error (NRMSE ≤. 8.8%) especially for sole cropped and intercropped chickpea. The soil N stocks were less adequately predicted (EF ≤ 0.28) with high relative error (NRMSE ≥ 56%) in sole cropping system, while it was moderately adequately predicted (EF ≤ 0.44) in intercropping. Under the two contrasted years and N-application conditions of this study, the temporal dynamic was well reproduced by the model for both plant and soil outputs with low simulation errors. RMSE values were lesser than 0.6 m2 m-2 (9%), 0.2 t ha-1 (14%) and 30 kg ha-1 (12%), respectively for LAI, grain yield and AGPN of sole cropped chickpea. The dynamic of soil water content was also well reproduced among all N-application rate and during the two cropping year, with RMSE equal to 27 mm (*<*10%). The present work provides the first calibration for chickpea sole crop and an evaluation for durum wheat-chickpea intercrops, which will allow to use the STICS model to simulate scenarios of innovative cropping practices based on crop diversification (i.e. grain legumes and cereals) and N-fertilization management.

In order to support new sustainable cropping systems, novel practices based on agro-ecological intensification are proposed at both field and farm scales ([Pansu et al., 2018](#page-15-0)). In this context, crop diversification (i.e. intercropping, cover crops and rotation) provide promising solutions to the problem of inadequate use of natural resources in agriculture ([Peoples](#page-15-0)  [et al., 2019](#page-15-0)), and that meet the growing objectives of production and environment ([Gaba et al., 2015)](#page-14-0). In particular, relying on facilitation and complementarity effects, cereal-legumes intercropping systems have demonstrated agro-environmental and socio-economic advantages compared to their corresponding sole crops in many previous studies ([Bedoussac et al., 2015;](#page-14-0) [Pelzer et al., 2012; Rodriguez et al., 2020)](#page-15-0). For example, N and water use efficiency were considerably increased in durum wheat grown as intercropped with chickpea ([Latati et al., 2019)](#page-15-0), forage pea [(Tribouillois et al., 2016](#page-15-0)) and faba bean ([Messaoudi et al.,](#page-15-0)  [2020)](#page-15-0).

Thanks to biological fixation of atmospheric N (N2), legumes are known to improve yield stability, N acquisition and water use when they are grown in intercropping more particularly with cereals ([Falconnier](#page-14-0) [et al., 2019](#page-14-0)). They can also reduce water pollution by decreasing nitrate (NO3) leaching under either common cover crop or intercropping practices ([Plaza-Bonilla et al., 2017)](#page-15-0). However, legumes are sensitive to various biotic and abiotic stress such as; (i) long drought period during reproductive stage [(Daryanto et al., 2017)](#page-14-0), (ii) frost damage at sowing stage which may have a drastic effect on seed lifting in particular for winter legumes ([Meyer and Badaruddin, 2001](#page-15-0)), (iii) low rate of N2 fixation due to phosphorus soil deficiency ([Houassine et al., 2019)](#page-14-0) and (iv) weed competition with legume crop that contribute to yield loss ([Mor](#page-15-0)[ishita, 2017](#page-15-0)). These limiting factors are directly responsible on yield instability when farmers adopt legumes in intercropping systems ([Latati](#page-15-0)  [et al., 2016)](#page-15-0). Furthermore, the lack of integrative diagnosis of agroecological indicators (i.e., biomass, yield, N yield, water balance and growth dynamic) is one of the most principal reasons that limit designing and evaluation of legumes-based intercropping systems ([Kherif et al., 2021; Watson et al., 2017](#page-15-0)).

Regarding diagnosis, soil-crop models provide a detailed view to better understand agro-environmental constrains that could affect crop resilience and sustainability ([Todd-Brown et al., 2012; Hayman et al.,](#page-15-0) [2010)](#page-15-0). They are also useful tools to design and evaluate innovative legumes-based cropping mixtures in terms of production and environmental outcomes and explore management options ([Kherif et al., 2020;](#page-14-0)  [Latati et al., 2019; Launay et al., 2009)](#page-14-0). Therefore, these models are able to incorporate the analysis of empirical data-set and simulate successfully the key agroecological processes (i.g. growth, yield formation, water and nutrients use) that need to be assessed in diversified systems (e.g. cover crop and intercropping). Since their adaptation to crop mixtures and especially intercropping, the use of crop models such as APSIM ([Holzworth et al., 2014)](#page-14-0), CROPSYS ([Singh et al., 2013](#page-15-0)), FASSET ([Berntsen et al., 2004)](#page-14-0) and STICS ([Brisson et al., 2004)](#page-14-0) to simulate water balance, nutrient acquisition and yield at different growth stages is increasingly reported in literature ([Ibrahim et al., 2016; Mal](#page-14-0)´ezieux et al., [2009)](#page-14-0). However, according to [Gaudio et al. (2019),](#page-14-0) modeling of annual crop mixtures is still under development and represents the main challenge for crop modelers given to difficulties of simulating the spatio-temporal heterogeneity and resources' partioning. Actual efforts to improve the ability of models to consider a wide range of crop mixtures is therefore essential and will allow both researchers and farmers to virtually explore different scenarios (crop species*soil*climate*practices) that are usually coast and time-consuming when tested in field experiments (Knorzer ¨ [et al., 2011)](#page-15-0).

STICS is one of the soil-crop models that are receiving considerable efforts to improve its predictive quality with regard to intercropping systems in the last years ([Justes et al., 2021; Paff et al., 2020](#page-14-0)). The daily time step simulation of crop growth by STICS provides a mechanistic

dynamic assessment of the agro-physiological processes that are controlled by cropping practices and pedo-climatic conditions ([Artru](#page-14-0)  [et al., 2017)](#page-14-0). This model was adapted to intercropping systems following a modeling approach based on an extension of sole crop models, representing two species in the system instead of one ([Brisson et al., 2004](#page-14-0)). Bi-specific versions were applied for assessing the effect of crop management in legume-cereals intercrops. This concerned especially the calibration of the STICS model on barley-pea [(Corre-Hellou et al., 2009;](#page-14-0)  [Launay et al., 2009)](#page-14-0), wheat-fescue ([Shili-Touzi et al., 2010)](#page-15-0) and wheat-pea intercropping ([Justes et al., 2021)](#page-14-0). Moreover, the model is being continuously adapted to new legume species such as soybean (J´[ego et al., 2010)](#page-14-0); faba bean ([Falconnier et al., 2019)](#page-14-0) and common bean ([Paleari et al., 2020)](#page-15-0). The STICS team is actually extending its work and partnerships to include new based-legume sole and intercropping systems from different pedoclimatic conditions in the model portfolio.

After water, N-soil availability is considered as the key driver that control growth and yield variability in cereals-legumes intercrops ([Naudin et al., 2010)](#page-15-0). N-application has been widely practiced to improve crop yield and biomass in the most of world agro-ecosystems over the last fifty years ([Cai et al., 2018)](#page-14-0). Nevertheless, farmers consider that N-application rate is difficult to adjust especially during intercropping management ([Malagoli et al., 2020)](#page-15-0). This was probably due to a wide range of factors such as; (i) the proportion of each species in the mixture, (ii) the importance of each intercropped species regardless farmer objectives and (iii) the variability in pedo-climatic conditions. Recent modeling works were recently performed to provide better decisions that can help farmers to optimize N application in intercropping management. In this context, AZODYN and AFISOL model has been recently evaluated for the first time under N-fertilization conditions in pea-wheat intercropping system ([Malagoli et al., 2020](#page-15-0)). This could help to better simulate a wide variability of N-fertilization strategies (e.g. optimal N dose for the best functioning of intercropping) and to design innovative and resilient legumes-based intercropping systems.

Regardless the strategic place of grain legumes in food production system, chcickpea (*Cicer arietinum* L) is the third most important grain legume cultivated in the word ([Martinelli et al., 2020)](#page-15-0). This species is considered as one of the major protein sources that can replace meat for the Mediterranean regions in particular for North African countries ([Cernay et al., 2015)](#page-14-0). Furthermore, chickpea-based intercropping system would contribute to achieve yield and N nutrition for the respective intercropped cereals thanks to biological N2 fixation ([Lal et al., 2017;](#page-15-0)  [Kherif et al., 2021)](#page-15-0). However, like the most of legume species, abiotic (drought, P-soil deficiency, high N-soil) and biotic (e.g. weed, pests and diseases) constraints could affect chickpea growth and N2 fixation especially under Mediterranean conditions were these limiting factors are more pounced ([Ryan et al., 2009)](#page-15-0).

This research paper focus on analyzing the response of durum wheatchickpea intercropping system to different levels of mineral N-fertilization based on a field experiment from northern Algeria and using a modeling approach. Our first objective was to calibrate and adapt the STICS model to chickpea crop grown under rain-fed conditions. This new and first calibration would allow STICS to predict the response of winter chickpea to different rates of N-application. In parallel, STICS was also calibrated for durum wheat using a common parameters set under the same conditions of soil, climate and crop management. The second objective was to test and analyze the robustness of STICS to simulate the intercropping system's performances (i.e. shoot and root growth, yield, N plant acquisition and water balance). By doing so, we explored the interest of the modeling approach to design and assess innovative strategies of N-fertilization (with combination of climate, soil and cropping system) that could provide a decision rules incerealslegumes intercropping management at the field scale.

## <span id="page-2-0"></span>**2. Materials and methods**

## *2.1. Climate and soil characteristics*

Data was collected from a field experiment carried out at an agronomic station in the Oued Smar region, northeast of Algiers (Algeria) (36◦42′ N, 3◦09′ E). This experiment was performed during two consecutive growing seasons (2018–2019 and 2019–2020). The climate can be described as Mediterranean sub-humid with an average yearly rainfall of 603 mm recorded during the last forty years. During this period, the annual temperature varied between 18.1 and 24.1 ◦C with an annual mean of 18.1 ◦C. (Table 1). This climate is also characterized by irregular rainfall in terms of frequency and quantity with long drought periods. The driest period interrupted only the second cropping season from the beginning to the end of March. There was a high contrast between the two growing seasons, in terms of rainfall and temperature. As that, the first cropping season (December. 2018 – June 2019) being wetter (total cumulated rainfall = 271 mm) and colder (average temperature = 14.2 ◦C) then the second cropping season (December. 2019 – June 2020) with cumulative rainfall of 189 mm and air temperature of 16.4 ◦C. Different soil characteristics such as sand, silt, and clay particules, and calcium carbonate (CaCO3) content, bulk density, and pH were measured at different soil depths (Table 1). Soil presented a silty clay loam texture at most of layers analyzed (from 0 to 100 cm). The soil of field experiment site was Verstisol, and presented quite homogenous conditions with no slope. The root growth is not restrained as the soil is deep and no physical constraints have been observed. The soil can be described as alkaline and non-calcareous, with deficiency for both available N and P. Soil parameters linked to physical and hydraulic characteristics were also measured as required for the STICS model such as bulk density and water content at permanent wilting point and field capacity (Table 1).

# *2.3. Plant and soil samplings and measurements*

Development stages for both crops were recorded with a particular attention to emergence (BBCH = 09) and flowering (BBCH = 65) dates. Initial soil samples were taken at the sowing date of each growing season which served for soil characterization and to measure initial soil water and mineral N (N-NH4 ++NO3 - ) content. Plant and soil samples were taken over five sampling periods during the first growing season. Three samplings were performed during the vegetative phase, one at the chickpea flowering stage (110 DAS), and the last sampling was done at harvest. In the second growing season three samplings periods were carried out, one during the vegetative phase, one at the flowering stage (107 DAS), while the last was done at harvest. At each sampling different variables of interest were measured such as: root depth, soil water and N content (at 0–20, 20–40, 40–60 and 60–80 cm), LAI, aboveground dry and wet biomass and N uptake by plant. At harvest grain yield, its components and the maximum plant height were measured. Soil moisture (at each 10 cm depth) was regularly measured by a soil probe (Diviner 2000, Sentek Pty Ltd, United States of America) for all plots with one observation per plot on a daily basis. The collected soil moisture values (in scaled frequency unit) from the probe measurements were calibrated (i.e. volumetric soil water content in m3 m-3 unit) according to methods reported by [Groves and Rose (2004)](#page-14-0) and [Haberland](#page-14-0)  [et al. (2014)](#page-14-0).

Physico-chemical analyses of the soil were performed through standard methods. Soil and plant N content were determined using Kjeldahl method ([Lynch and Barbano, 1999)](#page-15-0). Soil P content was measured with the Malachite green method after mixed digestion by both perchloric and nitric acids ([Valizadeh et al., 2003](#page-15-0)). The Anne Method [(McBratney](#page-15-0) [et al., 2000](#page-15-0)) was used to measure soil organic matter content. The Horton and Nelson method ([Leo, 1963)](#page-15-0) was performed to determine soil calcium carbonate content, while soil pH was measured in the soil suspension with deionized water (soil:water ratio = 1:2.5) with a pH meter ([Shen et al., 1996)](#page-15-0). The N acquired by plant in t ha-1 was estimated by multiplying dry biomass by the N concentration in the plant and subtracting initial seed N content from the whole biomass and grain N content. Climatic data were provided by the Dar Beida meteorological station (36◦72′ N, 3◦25′ E) which is located at 5.5 km from the experimental site.

#### *2.4. STICS model overview*

STICS is a daily time-step crop model in which phenology is mainly driven by thermal time ([Brisson et al., 2003)](#page-14-0). The plant is characterized by its shoot biomass and leaf area index ([Brisson et al., 2008](#page-14-0)). STICS simulates dynamically the establishment of the canopy, the biomass production and root development (including N and water absorption) ([Falconnier et al., 2019)](#page-14-0). Yield formation is decomposed into a grain setting process, which computes the number of produced grains, and a grain-filling process. These processes define the simulation of dry matter and N accumulated in the grain. The model also simulates the effects of N, water, and thermal stresses as well as anoxia. Intercropping systems can be modeled by STICS using a simple representation of the system divided into the dominant canopy and the understory canopy which is divided into a shaded and a sunlit part. Each canopy is defined by a light microclimate estimated by a radiative balance which drives the different dynamics of the three sub-systems ([Brisson et al., 2004)](#page-14-0). The model proposes two options for simulating radiation interception: (i) a direct application of the Beer's law, which is used for homogenous crops, and a radiation transfer approach, which is more suited for crop in rows (e.g. intercropping systems). For the crop water requirement, the model proposes a crop coefficient approach for which the potential evapotranspiration is defined as a driving variable. However, this approach is not suitable when the Beer's law could not be applied directly (i.e. use of the radiative transfer formalism). The alternative is a resistive approach which estimates plant water requirements and soil evaporation using the Shuttleworth and Wallace daily time-step model ([Brisson et al., 1998a](#page-14-0)).

## *2.5. Model inputs and simulation options*

STICS requires as inputs, soil and climate data, in addition to crop management and plant parameters. We set most of the soil parameters directly from the results of soil analysis [(Table 1)](#page-2-0), and/or by direct calculation in the case of some soil parameters (i.e. pedo-transfer function). We also parameterized both initial water and N content of the soil. Climate inputs include minimum and maximum air temperature, global radiation, rainfall, wind and relative humidity. For each combination of growing season (i.e. 2018/2019 and 2019/2020) × cropping system (i.e. sole wheat, sole chickpea, intercropped wheat and chickpea) we parameterized a crop management file (i.e. plant density, dates and depth of sowing and also the respective dates of N-application and irrigation). We considered a depth of 80 cm for the calculation of soil N and water stocks. This could lead to well calibration for other parameters (e.g. rooting depth). The harvest date was simulated based on seed water content. The emergence density was used as model input instead of sowing density in order to have a more realistic simulation. This last precaution was adopted to better take in consideration the impact of other factors (e.g. biotic stresses) that are not simulated by the model. Regardless the use of radiative transfer formalism during intercroppingsimulation, the inter-row width and the row orientation was parameterized. All treatments (combinations of cropping systems and N fertilizer rates) and cropping seasons were simulated for a total of 18 Units of Simulation (USMs).

The model was run using the JavaSTICS software which offers a userfriendly interface to manage the different inputs and outputs, forcing certain variables, and running simulations ([Brisson et al., 2003](#page-14-0)). The software also offers an optimization tool which is used in this study.

#### *2.6. STICS calibration*

The observed data in sole cropping systems were used for all the plant files calibration (i.e. sole chickpea, intercropped chickpea, sole wheat and intercropped wheat). The calibration was carried out using the data collected during both 2018–2019 and 2019–2020 growing seasons. To better assess the quality of the calibration, model evaluation (and not validation) was performed for all calibrated parameters. The temporal dynamic of all outputs variables in sole cropping system were evaluated among the same dataset used for calibration. However, the evaluation of intercropping was performed by using all data collected from intercropped plots in the first and second growing seasons. This makes the intercropping evaluation independent from the data used for its calibration.

# *2.6.1. Chickpea crop*

To our knowledge this work is the first calibration ever conducted for chickpea using the STICS crop model. This calibration procedure was performed using three steps, partially inspired from similar approach adopted on faba bean by [Falconnier et al. (2019).](#page-14-0) The first step consisted of a literature review for the identification of key parameters required by the model. The model was already calibrated for the pea crop. Due to the physiology similarities between the two species we conserved the same formalisms and initial values as the pea plant file ([Corre-Hellou et al.,](#page-14-0)  [2009)](#page-14-0), except for the LAI calculation option, for which we chose to simulate the LAI as the net balance between growth and senescence as fully described by [Brisson et al. (2008)](#page-14-0). We only set the *kmax* parameter from the literature ([Table 3](#page-5-0)) that corresponds to the maximum crop coefficient for water requirements. For chickpea species *kmax* corresponds to 1 according to the [Garofalo et al. (2009).](#page-14-0) In the second step, we set values of parameters directly from the experimental dataset. In total, 4 parameters were determined this way, including minimum and maximum number of fruits per surface area (i.e. *nbgrmin* and *nbgrmax*), maximum grain weight (*pgrainmaxi*) and maximum height of the crop (*hautmax*). The last step consisted of parameter optimization. This step followed the methodology presented by [Guillaume et al. (2011).](#page-14-0) These authors divided the optimization work into different stages, where each stage representing a key process simulated by the model. While the optimized values of the previous stage being used as the default values of the next stage.

We optimized several parameters, which were linked to the different processes taken into account by the model: phenology, roots growth, leaf expansion, shoot growth, partitioning of biomass in organs, N uptake, N fixation and yield formation ([Table 2](#page-4-0)). The optimized parameters in this present study are the most sensitive parameters of the model according to a sensitivity analysis conducted by [Ruget et al. (2002)](#page-15-0). Most calibration works using STICS use this sensitivity analysis as a basis ([Fal](#page-14-0)[connier el al, 2019)](#page-14-0) as well as the methodology we followed in this study and indicated by [Guillaume et al. (2011)](#page-14-0), this explain why we had not to carry out a sensitivity analysis before starting this work. Emergence parameters were not optimized as we forced the model to simulate plant emergence at each observed date. Furthermore, we did not optimize the parameters controlling the frost stress because no frost occurred during the field experiment. Parameter optimization was performed by using the following variables: (i) dates of observed development stages (end of juvenile phase, flowering and harvest stage), (ii) temporal dynamic of plant and soil variables (LAI, root depth, soil water and N contents (0–80 cm depth), above-ground dry biomass, and plant N content) and (iii) yield variables (number of grains per m2 , and grain yield). The optimization was carried out using the Javastics optimization tool, which uses the simplex algorithm ([Nelder-Mead, 1965](#page-15-0)), based on least squares criterion. It is a local and frequentist optimization method that minimizes a criterion in order to adequate the model with the observed values. The Nelder-Mead simplex is a non-linear algorithm used to optimize an objective function in a multidimensional space. The parameter values vary from a starting point and change during the multiple iterations until the values allow having a local optimum for the objective function. This method only needs the criterion value to ensure the optimization and is adapted to non-differentiable function, which is the case of STICS. As a heuristic method, the Nelder-Mead simplex permits to perform a quick optimization. However, the proposed solution may not be optimal as there is a risk to be trapped by local minima. Durum Wheat is one of the most common calibrated crops by STICS model. It has been calibrated for a wide range of cultivars by several studies ([Brisson et al., 1998b](#page-14-0) and 2002). # *2.6.3. Durum wheat-chickpea Intercropping*

The intercropping calibration was carried out using the sole crops datasets of each species, while the associated crops dataset was only used for model evaluation. The initial values of the plant files correspond to the final values obtained during sole crops calibration. The STICS intercrop model assumes that a simple geometric shape (i.e. rectangle or triangle) can represent a canopy ([Brisson et al., 2008)](#page-14-0). We tested both shapes for the two crops, and the triangle shape gave the best results for both wheat and chickpea canopies. The optimization and simulation procedures of intercropping system were performed by using the radiative transfer formalism. We developed one supplementary stage in optimization procedures for intercrops by using LAI observed values. The optimized parameters that were generated from these procedures were: *ktrou*, *rapforme*, *adfol*, *dfolbas* and *dfolhaut* (see Table 2). The *rsmin*  parameter was also optimized since it is a part of the resistive approach formalism that was activated to simulate crop water requirements in intercropping. The potential evapotranspiration was calculated using the Shuttleworth-Wallace equation.

# *2.7. Statistical analysis and model evaluation*

We evaluated the model both graphically and quantitatively using several statistical indicators: the Root Mean Square Error (RMSE), the Normalized Root Mean Square Error Model (NRMSE), the model Efficiency (EF), and the Pearson correlation coefficient (R2 ). RMSE (Eq. (1)) allows determining the model prediction error, we try to minimize this value as much as possible to maximize the accuracy of the model.

$$\text{RMSE} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{n}} \sum \text{ni} = 1 \text{ (Oi} - \text{Si)}^2 \tag{1}$$

NRMSE (Eq. (2)) is the normalized RMSE, which facilitates the comparison when considering different scales.

$$\text{NRMSE} = \frac{\text{RMSE}}{\overline{\text{O}}} \ast 100\tag{2}$$

EF (Eq. (3)) is a valuable indicator to compare model simulations with different parameters set

$$EF = 1 - \frac{\sum n\text{ }i = 1(Oi-\text{Si})^2}{\sum ni = 1(Oi-\text{Si})^2} \tag{3}$$

R2 (Eq. (4)) is used to evaluate the accuracy of the linear regression between observed and simulated values.

$$\mathcal{R}^2 = \quad \left[\frac{\sum \text{n i} = 1 \quad (\text{Si} \text{i} - \text{ }\overline{\text{Si}})(\text{Oi} - \text{ }\overline{\text{Oi}})}{\sigma \text{S} \sigma \text{O}}\right]^2 \tag{4}$$

With Oi and Si being respectively the observed and simulated values for the ith measurements. O and S being the mean of the observed and simulated values, and n the total number of observations. The <span id="page-5-0"></span>combination of these four indicators permitted a detailed evaluation of the model. We used the STICS *eval R* library to compute all plots and statistical criteria presented in this study.

#### *4.2. The credibility in reproducing plant and soil variables dynamic*

The main performance of a soil-crop model is not only related to the overall accuracy of model predictions. It also depends on the modelability to reproduce the temporal dynamics of soil and plant variables under a wide range of conditions ([Kherif et al., 2020; Wallach et al.,](#page-14-0)  [2014)](#page-14-0). One of the main novelty of this work was to assess the behavior and credibility of the STICS model by analyzing how the main output variables change among contrasted cropping system (i.e. intercropping and sole crop) and N-availability (N rates: N-30, N-60 and N-100). The results show that the temporal dynamic was well reproduced for plant outputs according to the new calibration of chickpea crop. As that, the predicted effect of low, moderate and high N rate on LAI, grain yield and AGPN was satisfactory agreed with the observed one for sole cropped chickpea. However, prediction errors were better minimized under moderate and high levels of N-fertilization in both 2018–2019 and 2019–2020 growing seasons. They were lesser than 0.6 m2 m-2 (9%), 0.2 t ha-1 (14%) and 30 kg ha-1 (12%), respectively for LAI, grain yield and AGPN ([Figs. 4 and 5](#page-9-0)). The dynamic of soil water content was also well reproduced among all N-application rates and during the two cropping year. As a result, RMSE was lesser than 27 mm (*<*10%). The same trends were observed for sole cropped wheat during the second growing season with very low (*<*5%) prediction error, especially for grain yield variable [(Fig. 6](#page-11-0)). The STICS was also well evaluated on both plant and soil outputs (i.e. AGB, grain yield, AGPN and soil water and N stock) and for both intercropped species. For example, in the first year of experiment (2018–2019) and under moderate N-fertilization ([Fig. 7)](#page-12-0); the temporal dynamic was generally well reproduced for the most of variable of interest. Furthermore, the lower values of prediction error were observed for intercropped chickpea. This was probably due to the new calibration that was performed adequately on chickpea crop. These results are in line with these reported on modeling N and light sharing in pea-wheat intercrops ([Malagoli et al., 2020](#page-15-0)). This recent research demonstrated that AZODYN and AFISOL model were able to well-simulate the temporal dynamics of different soil-crop variables under different scenarios of N fertilization.

# *4.3. STICS as a robust tool to design and assess innovative cropping system*

The satisfactory results obtained in this calibration work under contrasting climatic and N fertilization conditions, confirm the STICS robustness that was reported previously for 15 calibrated species ([Coucheney et al., 2015)](#page-14-0). Our main results demonstrate that the STICS model could accurately reproduce a wide range of dynamic variables at the same time and extend it to new species (i.e. chickpea), new cropping systems (i.e. durum wheat-chickpea intercrop) and new variables (i.e. root depth). The results show also that the model performs well in simulating soil variables especially water content up to an 80 cm depth under contrasted climatic conditions. However, these variables were simulated only on the first 30 cm depth by most of previous calibration studies [(Paleari et al., 2020; Falconnier et al., 2019; Coucheney et al.,](#page-15-0) [2015)](#page-15-0). These findings demonstrate the credibility and robustness of STICS in diagnosing either diversified or monocropped systems. The model can consequently be applied to studies analyzing the effect of climate change ([Falconnier et al., 2020)](#page-14-0). STICS was also considered as a strong tool to assess the resilience and sustainability of innovative cropping systems by simulating a wide range of key agro-environmental indicators (i.e. water drainage and nitrate leaching) ([Plaza-Bonilla et al.,](#page-15-0) [2015)](#page-15-0). The successful calibration of STICS in this study will offer the opportunity to design and evaluate innovative based-chickpea and other legumes cropping systems in the Mediterranean rain-fed conditions. As compared to tropical, drought and low N-soil availability are considered as the main stress factors of the yield variability in Mediterranean region, when a good control of pest pressure is applied ([Karrou and Oweis,](#page-14-0)  [2012)](#page-14-0). The model was able to reproduce efficiently plant variables for both the sole crops and intercropping despite contrasting climatic condition (i.e. two different growing years), and different N fertilization rates. Hence, we could use STICS to test different N fertilization strategies, especially for the intercropping where N fertilization remains poorly documented. Thus, the confirmation that the STICS crop model could simulate correctly under our field conditions provides a useful tool that could help farmers during N-fertilization decision-making on cereals-legumes intercropping systems. Previous calibration works gave good results in simulating intercropping systems (i.e. pea-barley intercrop) but did not consider plant height as one of the main factors that define light sharing as the two species have a similar height growth ([Corre-Hellou, 2009](#page-14-0)). In our study, intercropping was well calibrated despite the fact that wheat and chickpea presented consequent differences in terms of plant height. This may be explained by the low prediction error (RMSE ranged from 0.01 to 0.1 m) obtained for the height variable. Plant height calibration was crucial to accurately simulate radiation interception and microclimate in intercropping ([Launay et al.,](#page-15-0)  [2009)](#page-15-0). Furthermore, the model performed well during the two growing seasons despite the fact that the sowing orientation was highly contrasted. As the row orientation affect the shaded parts in intercropping, our results are in good agreement with these reported by [Artru et al.](#page-14-0) [(2017)](#page-14-0) on STICS robustness under contrasted shade conditions. This would be useful to simulate the (i) sowing delays for both mixed crop ([Launay et al., 2009)](#page-15-0), (ii) different planting structure (i.e. inter-row and orientation) and (iii) to evaluate different light capture conditions, in order to design diversified cropping system. This first STICS calibration study was mainly performed on sole cropped chickpea and associated with the model evaluation on intercropping. It will be useful to simulate the main performance of wheat-chickpea intercropping for which the literature remains scarce, in order to improve this innovative cropping system.

#### **5. Conclusion**

Based on an intercropping experiment under three contrasting N fertilization rates, we successfully calibrated the STICS soil-crop model for chickpea crop under rain-fed Mediterranean conditions. This is a main originality of the present research and which was associated by the first evaluation of STICS model on chickpea-based intercropping system. Among a complete calibration based principally on transparent optimization method, most of model parameters were directly optimized from field measured variables. The optimized parameters among all included crop processes (i.e. emergence, plant growth, crop development, yield formation and N uptake) led to accurate and satisfactory calibration of soil and crop variables. High accuracy in calibration was statistically (i.e. EF, NRMSE, RMSE, R2 and MD) confirmed for most variables of interest, except for chickpea grain yield. The poor performance in calibration of chickpea grain yield was probably due to biotic factors such as yield loss at previous to harvest due to bird damages. Prediction errors relative to aboveground biomass, LAI, grain yield and N uptake by plant were quite similar with lower range, to the ones found by other STICS-calibration studies. However, we found that the prediction errors for soil variables (soil water and N content) were at the lower range to these obtained by several studies. Furthermore, our results illustrate that the performed calibration was able to capture the temporal dynamics of the most studied variables in contrasted situations (i.e. cropping systems, growing seasons and N-application rates). Finally, this calibration study will allow the STICS model the ability to well simulate innovative cropping practices based on crop diversification (grain legumes and cereals) and N-fertilization management. A key perspective now, to better benefit from this research study, is to extent this new calibrated version (chickpea-based intercropping system) of STICS model involving the simulation of a wide diversity of pedoclimatic or cropping practice situations (i.e. innovative and conventional cropping system). This would be in particular very helpful to test and assess STICS robustness and credibility in even more contrasted <span id="page-14-0"></span>conditions all over the world.
