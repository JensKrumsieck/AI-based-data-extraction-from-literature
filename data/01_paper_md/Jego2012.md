# a b s t r a c t

Using remote sensing (RS) data to re-initialize input parameters that are not readily available is considered an effective way to improve regional-scale crop modelling. Our objective is to evaluate the conditions of application of the re-initialization (e.g., number of image acquisitions, spatial resolution of the homogeneous field subunits, etc.). Although several aspects of the coupling between crop models and RS data have been investigated, extensive verification using multiple years is required prior to implementation of the approach at the regional scale, in order to evaluate its robustness and consistency in response to climatic variations. We evaluated the performance of STICS, a functional crop model, in predicting yield and biomass by using Leaf Area Index (LAI) retrieved from RS data to re-initialize selected input parameters over ten growing seasons (1999–2008) for rainfed corn, soybean and spring wheat fields cultivated near Ottawa (Ontario, Canada). The impact of the number of RS images and of the definition of the homogeneous spatial units required to re-initialize input parameters was also evaluated. Green LAI was estimated with the modified transformed vegetation index (MTVI2) using either airborne hyperspectral data (Compact Airborne Spectrographic Imager, CASI) or more readily available multispectral satellite data (Landsat TM and SPOT). Re-initialization of the model parameters involves using the simplex optimization algorithm to minimize the weighted sum-of-squared differences between LAI retrieved from RS and LAI estimated by the crop model.

Yield and biomass predictions were greatly improved through the re-initialization of seeding date, seeding density and field capacity. Almost no bias was observed, and the relative root mean square error (RMSE%) was 13% for yield and 23% for biomass (versus 22% and 44% without optimization). The improvement in model predictions was particularly noticeable in the case of water-stress conditions or a deficit of growing degree-days, indicating that the method is sensitive to climate variability. The results were very close to the yield and biomass predictions (i.e., RMSE% of 11% for yield and 17% for biomass) obtained with actual management and soil properties. Most of the improved predictions were associated with re-initialization of the seeding date. When only two LAI values were used to re-initialize the seeding date, the RMSE% values for yield and biomass predictions were 15% and 27%, respectively. Finally, we showed that overlaying field boundaries onto soil texture was sufficient to accurately predict yields. The addition of a third layer, based on LAI-homogeneous zones, did not improve yield predictions because the model was not able to capture some of the small within-field yield variations (<0.5 t ha−1). # **1. Introduction**

To increase sustainability in crop production, regional tools need to be developed to help producers and their advisors better manage fields. These tools can provide them with spatial crop biomass and yield monitoring and simulation capability for evaluating the impact of beneficial management practices. Moreover, reliable yield forecasts and predictions toward the end ofthe growing season are useful for planning grain handling operations and forecasting market prices. Modelling crop growth is a promising approach for estimating yield and biomass. It can provide data to explain and gauge the main abiotic limiting factors leading to crop yield reductions; this information can be useful for producers and crop insurance companies. However, the application of crop models at the regional scale is often limited by uncertainty associated with the actual values of some of the input parameters. In order to reduce this uncertainty, plant and cultivar parameters need first to be calibrated for the regional growing conditions. Soil properties can usually be retrieved from soil databases covering large areas of farmland in many countries. However, they might be not sufficient

to fill in all the soil parameters needed by the crop model. Information on crop identification and management practices is often not available and is therefore an important source of uncertainty for regional crop modelling. Multi-temporal remote sensing (RS) data can assist in obtaining this information, provided that the spatial resolution of the satellite data is compatible with the size of the fields and soil heterogeneity.

Re-initialization of crop models using Leaf Area Index (LAI) values derived from RS data is one of the most established methods of coupling crop models and RS (e.g., [Maas,](#page-11-0) [1988,](#page-11-0) [1992;](#page-11-0) [Barnes](#page-11-0) et [al.,](#page-11-0) [1997;](#page-11-0) [Guérif](#page-11-0) [and](#page-11-0) [Duke,](#page-11-0) [2000;](#page-11-0) [Prévot](#page-11-0) et [al.,](#page-11-0) [2003;](#page-11-0) [Boegh](#page-11-0) et [al.,](#page-11-0) [2004;](#page-11-0) [Dente](#page-11-0) et [al.,](#page-11-0) [2008;](#page-11-0) [Fang](#page-11-0) et [al.,](#page-11-0) [2008).](#page-11-0) Several crop models, such as CERES ([Ritchie](#page-11-0) [and](#page-11-0) [Otter,](#page-11-0) [1984;](#page-11-0) [Barnes](#page-11-0) et [al.,](#page-11-0) [1997),](#page-11-0) EPIC [(Williams](#page-11-0) et [al.,](#page-11-0) [1984),](#page-11-0) and STICS ([Brisson](#page-11-0) et [al.,](#page-11-0) [1998,](#page-11-0) [2002,](#page-11-0) [2003),](#page-11-0) have the capacity to use measured LAIto re-initialize input parameters. Before a crop model can be used for a given agro-environment, calibration and performance evaluation must be undertaken to assess the model predictions. [Dente](#page-11-0) et [al.(2008)](#page-11-0) showed the importance of calibrating a crop model for a cultivar used in the area of interest. [Jégo](#page-11-0) et [al.](#page-11-0) [(2010,](#page-11-0) [2011)](#page-11-0) showed that, after calibration of plant and cultivar parameters, the STICS model was able to predict LAI, biomass, and yield with acceptable accuracy using one cultivar of spring wheat and soybean and two cultivars of corn, the three main crops grown in the Mixedwood Plains ecozone of eastern Canada, which extends over 3◦ of latitude and 10◦ of longitude. The STICS crop model also has a built-in capability to optimize input parameters by minimizing the difference between calculated output variables (e.g., LAI) and measured variables.

For the re-initialization of crop model parameters, it is important to define a subset of key parameters to be adjusted and to determine the critical time frame for acquisition of the target variables to be assimilated. If crop and cultivar parameters have been previously calibrated, only management parameters and soil parameters can be optimized. [Dente](#page-11-0) et [al.](#page-11-0) [(2008)](#page-11-0) identified three parameters to be optimized in the CERES-Wheat crop model: seeding date, soil field capacity and wilting point moisture content. In using the CERES-Maize model for corn yield predictions, [Fang](#page-11-0) et [al.](#page-11-0) [(2008)](#page-11-0) showed that seeding date, plant density and nitrogen (N) fertilization are the key parameters that can be adjusted through the assimilation of LAI retrieved from RS data. In the STICS model, [Prévot](#page-11-0) et [al.](#page-11-0) [(2003)](#page-11-0) identified seeding date, soil moisture at field capacity, and a parameter controlling soil evaporation as candidates for optimization. A sensitivity analysis of the STICS model by [Ruget](#page-11-0) et [al.](#page-11-0) [(2002)](#page-11-0) showed that LAI was also strongly sensitive to plant density (at least for corn and winter wheat).

LAI can be operationally derived from visible and near-infrared (VIS and NIR) data using vegetation indices (e.g., [Wiegand](#page-11-0) et [al.,](#page-11-0) [1979;](#page-11-0) [Myneni](#page-11-0) et [al.,](#page-11-0) [1995;](#page-11-0) [Price](#page-11-0) [and](#page-11-0) [Bausch,](#page-11-0) [1995).](#page-11-0) Because the widely used normalized difference vegetation index (NDVI) [(Rouse](#page-11-0) et [al.,](#page-11-0) [1974;](#page-11-0) [Tucker](#page-11-0) et [al.,](#page-11-0) [1985;](#page-11-0) [Myneni](#page-11-0) et [al.,](#page-11-0) [1995)](#page-11-0) exhibits saturation at a relatively low LAI level and sensitivity to soil background reflectance, other indices have been developed to address some of its weaknesses (e.g., for multispectral data: EVI/EVI2, [Huete](#page-11-0) et [al.,](#page-11-0) [1997,](#page-11-0) [1999,](#page-11-0) [2002;](#page-11-0) WDRVI, [Gitelson,](#page-11-0) [2004;](#page-11-0) for hyperspectral data: MTVI2, [Haboudane](#page-11-0) et [al.,](#page-11-0) [2004,](#page-11-0) which minimizes sensitivity to chlorophyll). Acquiring cloud-free images from an optical satellite can be a challenge. Satellites with short revisit intervals, such as MODIS (Moderate Resolution Imaging Spectroradiometer) and MERIS (Medium Spectral Resolution Imaging Spectrometer), offer more opportunities for cloud-free acquisitions. They have been used for assimilation of LAI in crop models in regions with large cultivated fields. However,the large pixel size (250 m to 1 km) ofthese data could limit the usefulness of data assimilation for other agricultural regions because (1)it precludes running the crop model for small crop fields and could bias the predictions when mixed pixels are used;(2)itis not straightforward to compare model predictions at this scale with actual values (e.g., LAI, yield, biomass) measured in the field; and (3) while agricultural recommendations and policies are usually made atthe regional level,they are implemented by producers at either the field or the homogeneous subunit scale. In contrast, the higher spatial resolution sensors (e.g., 30-m pixel for Landsat TM or 20-m pixel for SPOT-4 HRV-IR) can provide relevant data for small fields but are often limited by their long revisit time and low spectral resolution. These issues will be mostly overcome by the launch, starting in 2013, of Sentinel-2 satellites, which have narrower wave bands, a 5-day revisittime, and 10–60 m pixel sizes.

The aim of this work is to evaluate the conditions of application of the re-initialization in order to establish a procedure for predicting biomass and yield over agricultural regions of eastern Canada. The following hypotheses were tested for evaluating the impact of the re-initialization procedure on the model performance: (1) biomass and yield predictions are significantly improved when at least two images are used, (2) this improvement is particularly significant for crops grown under suboptimal climatic conditions and (3) there is no significant model prediction improvement by defining LAI-homogeneous field subunits compared to those defined from field boundaries overlaid with soiltexture map. In preparation for Sentinel-2, we used a combination of Landsat, Compact Airborne Spectrographic Imager (CASI) and SPOT images collected for 10 years over an experimental farm close to Ottawa (ON, Canada) to quantify the performance improvement of yield and biomass predictions for corn, soybean and spring wheat obtained by reinitializing the STICS crop model using LAI derived from RS data for a wide range of climatic conditions. The impact of the number of RS images, the acquisition windows and the definition of spatial units on which simulations are run are evaluated from the standpoint of the performance of yield and biomass predictions at field scale.

## **2. Materials and methods**

Model predictions with re-initialization based on either two LAI values (2-LAI) or all available LAI values (All-LAI) were compared with model predictions using actual (Actual) and recommended (Default) field practices ([Table](#page-2-0) 1). The management practices and soil property data collected for each field were used in the actual simulation, whereas the recommended management practices and soil properties extracted from a regional soil database were used as initial conditions in the other simulations. Finally, the yield predictions obtained from a last set of simulations at a finer spatial scale (HighRes) were evaluated to determine whether or not it is useful to increase the number of spatial simulation units by delineating LAI-homogeneous zones.

## 2.2. LAI retrieval from remote sensing data

Depending on the year, between 3 and 9 cloud-free optical images were acquired each year from 1999 to 2008. These images were from different sources: the SPOT and Landsat multi-spectral satellite sensors, and the Compact Airborne Spectrographic Imager (CASI) hyperspectral sensor. The spatial resolution was 30 m for Landsat images, 20 m for SPOT images, and 2 m for CASI images. All images were radiometrically calibrated, andthenprocessed withan atmospheric correction to obtain surface reflectance using the 6S model for Landsat and SPOT data [(Vermote](#page-11-0) et [al.,](#page-11-0) [1997)](#page-11-0) and CAM5S for the CASI data [(O'Neill](#page-11-0) et [al.,](#page-11-0) [1997).](#page-11-0) Geometric corrections were made to co-register all the images to the same coordinate system without spatial re-sampling. The images were then used to calculate the MTVI2 vegetation index ([Haboudane](#page-11-0) et [al.,](#page-11-0) [2004)](#page-11-0) and to estimate LAI following the approach of [Baret](#page-11-0) [and](#page-11-0) [Guyot](#page-11-0) [(1991).](#page-11-0)

Median LAI values were extracted from the LAI map for each simulation unit. We selected median values because they are less sensitive to outliers than mean LAI values. LAI outliers appeared mainly at the edge of the fields where geometric correction was not perfect and mixed pixels were present owing to the different land cover types. In addition, trees and pylons may be present in the fields and hence may interfere with LAI retrievals from RS data.

# 2.4. Procedure for re-initializing STICS crop model using remotely sensed LAI

STICS has a built-in capability to use LAI from external sources such as RS data. LAI can be used as a driving variable or to reinitialize input parameters. Driving the model with LAI requires daily LAI values from emergence to harvest. We did not use this approach because RS data are not available at this temporal resolution. Concerning the re-initializing approach, n LAI values (at least two values) are needed to re-initialize n−1 input parameters. Re-initialization of the STICS model was carried out by optimizing the model input parameters until the temporal dynamics of the model-simulated LAI were in best agreement with the multitemporal LAI retrieved from RS data. Parameter optimization is based on the simplex algorithm [(Nelder](#page-11-0) [and](#page-11-0) [Mead,](#page-11-0) [1965;](#page-11-0) [Lagarias](#page-11-0) et [al.,](#page-11-0) [1996),](#page-11-0) which has been widely used for optimization procedures. This method allows the optimization of several parameters using one or several target variables. The statistical criterion to be minimized is the weighted sum-of-squared differences between the modeled LAI and LAI retrieved from RS. The range of variation of the optimized parameters was constrained through an input file to avoid unrealistic values. The seeding dates fell between mid-April and the end of May for spring wheat and the first of May and mid-June for corn and soybean. Seeding density varied between 300 and 400 seeds m−2 for spring wheat, between 30 and 50 seeds m−2 for soybean, and between 5 and 9 seeds m−2 for corn. The range of soil moisture values at field capacity was set at ±15% ofthe initial value.

# 2.5. Procedure for defining parameters to be reinitialized

Before assimilating LAI into the crop model, itis criticalto determine which parameters are relevant for re-initialization. Previous studies on the STICS crop model showed that LAI values for winter wheat and corn are highly sensitive to seeding date, seeding density, and soil moisture at field capacity (which allows to vary the amount of water available to plants) [(Ruget](#page-11-0) et [al.,](#page-11-0) [2002;](#page-11-0) [Prévot](#page-11-0) et [al.,](#page-11-0) [2003).](#page-11-0) We performed additional analyses in order to confirm these results using the spring wheat, corn and soybean cultivars adapted in STICS for growing conditions of eastern Canada by [Jégo](#page-11-0) et [al.](#page-11-0) [(2010,](#page-11-0) [2011).](#page-11-0) We evaluated the impact of three management parameters (seeding date, seeding density, and seeding depth) and three soil parameters (field capacity, soil depth, and bulk density) on LAI, biomass and yield for the three crops studied. Because most of the crops in eastern Canada are rainfed, crop water stress may impact LAI, biomass growth, and final yield production. As demonstrated in previous studies ([Ruget](#page-11-0) et [al.,](#page-11-0) [2002;](#page-11-0) [Prévot](#page-11-0) et [al.,](#page-11-0) [2003;](#page-11-0) [Dente](#page-11-0) et [al.,](#page-11-0) [2008),](#page-11-0) soil field capacity is a useful parameter for modulating the frequency and intensity of crop water stress in the model. In addition to field capacity, we evaluated the impact of soil depth and bulk density. For these sensitivity analyses, we used the 2001 climatic year, which included a potential water-stress period during late summer due to low levels of precipitation.

As for the definition of the constraints of the optimized parameters, the tested values fell within the range of variation for observations relevantto the Mixedwood Plains ecozone. The tested seeding dates ranged from 20 April to 10 May for spring wheat and from 1 May to 25 May for corn and soybean using 1-day increments. The seeding date affected the temporal dynamics of LAI and the final yield. For example, with the same parameter setting, delaying the seeding date by 10 days caused a yield decrease of 1.0 t ha−1 for spring wheat, 0.6 t ha−1 for corn, and 0.5 t ha−1 for soybean. Seeding density varied between 300 and 400 seeds m−2 for spring wheat, between 30 and 50 seeds m−2 for soybean, and between 5 and 9 seeds m−2 for corn, with increments of 10, 2 and 1 seeds m−2 respectively. Seeding density had little impact on spring wheat LAI and final yield; it had a greater impact on corn and soybean. For corn, increasing seeding density from 5 to 9 seeds m−2 increased LAI by 1.6 and final yield by 0.8 t ha−1. For soybean, increasing seeding density from 30 to 50 seeds m−2 increased LAI by 1.0 and final yield by 0.5 t ha−1. We varied seeding depth from 1 to 6 cm using 1-cm increments. Seeding depth did not have a significant impact on LAI or the final yield of the three crops. The tested range of soil moisture values at field capacity was set at ±15% of the initial value (for a loamy soil), which corresponds to a soil water capacity ranging from 75 to 240 mm over a 1-m soil depth. Five soil depths from 0.60 to 1.00 m and nine bulk densities (from 1.1 to 1.5 g cm−3 with 0.05 steps) were tested. The results confirmed that LAI was highly sensitive to field capacity. The range of soil depth and bulk density values tested had almost no impact on LAI.

In summary, seeding date, seeding density, and field capacity were chosen as the key input parameters to be re-initialized through the data optimization procedure. The results of this preliminary work confirmed that corn and soybean LAI and final yield werehighly sensitive to seedingdate andseedingdensity. The seeding date affected the temporal dynamics of LAI, and seeding density mainly affected maximum LAI. Early LAI values, obtained during the fast growth period, can thus be used to re-initialize the seeding date. For spring wheat, seeding date was the parameter with the greatest influence on LAI.

AtleastfourRS images, well distributed across a growing season, are required to re-initialize these three parameters. These results are consistent with those of [Dente](#page-11-0) et [al.](#page-11-0) [(2004,](#page-11-0) [2008)](#page-11-0) and [Fang](#page-11-0) et [al.](#page-11-0) [(2008)](#page-11-0) for the assimilation of LAI retrieved from RS into the CERES-wheat and CERES-maize models.

# 2.6. Simulation units

Simulation units were defined by overlaying field boundaries onto the soil texture map [(Fig.](#page-5-0) 1). This allowed us to run the model on units with uniform soil properties and crop management. Fields 2, 3, 8, 9, 11, 13, 14, 14NE, 16 and 17 have only one soil texture (1 simulation unit for each field), while fields 5, 23 and 25 have two soil textures (two simulations units for each field), and field 4 has three soil textures (3 simulation units). Based on these 19 simulation units, four sets of simulations were performed. For all simulations, the STICS model was initialized on 15 April with soil moisture at field capacity and an average soil mineral N content of 50 kg N ha−1. Harvest date was calculated by the model based on grain moisture (14% for wheat, 13% for soybean, and 15.5% for corn). Input data files were created using data from the local weather station.

The first set of simulations (labeled Actual) consisted of the simulations using the actual management and soil properties without RS data [(Table](#page-2-0) 1). Because information on management and soil properties is not always readily available at a regional scale, we performed a second set of simulations (labeled Default) using the management practices defined in the recommendations of the

Ontario Ministry of Agriculture, Food, and Rural Affairs [(OMAFRA,](#page-11-0) [2002)](#page-11-0) for the study area, and the average soil properties calculated from a regional soil database. In these simulations, spring wheat was assumed to be planted on 20 April at 350 seeds m−2; and soybean and corn were planted on 10 May at 40 and 7 seeds m−2, respectively. The performance assessment was carried out by comparing the LAI, biomass and yield prediction performance of the three simulation methods (Default, 2-LAI and All-LAI) with the corresponding predictions derived from the actual simulation and with field measurements. This last set of simulations (HighRes) was performed to evaluate the yield prediction performance in LAI-homogeneous zones obtained by segmentation of the LAI map. The segmentation was performed using a multi-resolution segmentation approach implemented in eCognition software, with weighting factors of 0.9 and 0.1 for the spectral and the shape heterogeneity, and a scale factor of 2 [(Benz](#page-11-0) et [al.,](#page-11-0) [2004).](#page-11-0) In all, 57 simulation units were defined based on this segmentation. Predicted yields were compared with the measured yield. These comparisons were performed only for a subset of fields for which yields maps were available, and for which N fertilization was homogeneous and close to recommended rates [(Table](#page-2-0) 3). Delineation of LAI-homogeneous zones was done using the image closest to the maximum LAI at a time just before the beginning of leaf senescence, because this is when maximum variability in growing conditions is expressed in the LAI. This period corresponds to late August for corn, the beginning of August for soybean, and the beginning of July for spring wheat. The images should incorporate most ofthe stresses that can affect final biomass and yield, and they maximize the potential differences in LAI values due to these stresses.

# 2.7. Statistical criteria

Three statistical criteria were used for evaluating the model performance [(Smith](#page-11-0) et [al.,](#page-11-0) [1996):](#page-11-0)

Model efficiency (EF): optimal value = 1

$$EF = 1 - \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} \text{(Pi} - \text{Oi})^2}{\sum_{i=1}^{n} \text{(Oi} - \text{\textdegree O})^2}$$

Mean error (ME) and its relative value in % (ME%): optimal value = 0

$$ME = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} (\text{Oi} - \text{Pi}); \text{ME\%} = \left(\frac{ME}{\bar{\text{O}}}\right) \times 100$$

root mean square error (RMSE) and its relative value (RMSE%): optimal value = 0

$$RMSE = \sqrt{\frac{1}{n} \sum_{n=1}^{n} (\text{Oi} - \text{Pi})^2}; \text{ RMSE\%} = \left(\frac{\text{RMSE}}{\bar{\text{O}}}\right) \times 100$$

where n is the number of observations, Oi the observed value, O¯ the mean of the observed values, and Pi the value predicted by the model.

[Jamieson](#page-11-0) et [al.](#page-11-0) [(1991)](#page-11-0) provided an estimate of the accuracy of simulations using theRMSE%value.Asimulation is considered to be excellent when RMSE% ≤ 10%, good when 10% < RMSE% ≤ 20%, fair when 20% < RMSE% ≤ 30%, and poor when RMSE% > 30%. A model with an efficiency level higher than 0.6 is generally considered efficient.

### 3.2. 2-LAI simulations

Model predictions were improved by using two LAI values to optimize seeding date [(Fig.](#page-6-0) 2d–f), but scattering remained high for biomass and LAI predictions for spring wheat and soybean (RMSE% > 30%; [Table](#page-7-0) 6a and b). Better LAI and biomass predictions were obtained for corn (RMSE% of 35.5% for LAI and 19.5% for biomass). Yield predictions [(Table](#page-7-0) 6c) were also improved, and were good for all three crops (RMSE% < 15%). However, as illustrated by Figs. 3 and 4 and [Table](#page-7-0) 6d, the adjusted seeding dates were poor for all three crops when compared with actual seeding dates (RMSE about 12 days). The adjusted seeding dates may incorporate impacts from other constraints, such as plant density and soil moisture at field capacity, causing them to deviate from the actual dates.

#### 3.3. All-LAI simulations

When the three parameters (seeding date, plant density, and soil moisture at field capacity) were optimized simultaneously [(Fig.](#page-6-0) 2g–i), LAI and biomass predictions were good for corn and spring wheat (RMSE% < 30%; [Table](#page-7-0) 6a and b). For soybean, although LAI was well predicted (RMSE% = 26.1%), biomass was slightly overestimated (ME% = 25.6% and RMSE% = 40.5%). This overestimation was partly due to leaf shedding during senescence. In Field 14 in 2008, the optimized seeding date was 20 days earlier than the actual date, and, consequently, even if maximum LAI and final biomass were correctly predicted, other values during the growing season were shifted by about 20 days. A comparison showed that LAI retrieved from RS data was much higher than the ground LAI measurements at the beginning of the growing season. A more in-depth investigation showed thatthe field was severely contaminated with weeds and regrowth of the previous crop (canola) at the beginning of the growing season, and this vegetation was probably captured in the RS observations, leading to an LAI estimate much higher than the actual soybean LAI. This represented an additional source of bias in the model predictions. Overall, the model predictions using all available LAI values were accurate for all three crops and very close to the simulations using actual management practices and measured soil properties (i.e., without RS data) [(Fig.](#page-6-0) 2j–l). The predicted biomass values were not as good as those obtained from the actual simulations, but the LAI predictions were better. This was expected since the optimization procedure minimizes the difference between observed and predicted LAI, butit confirms that the parameters chosen for optimization had a considerable impact on LAI dynamics, and thus were relevant for re-initialization. The temporal dynamics of LAI were also well predicted by the model when all RS LAI were used (Fig. 3). When only two LAI values were used, there were some discrepancies due to the lack of information about maximum LAI (e.g., 1999 soybean experiment) or a slight time lag between RS LAI and field LAI at the beginning of the growing season (e.g., 2000 corn experiment). Another concern relates to the difficulty of capturing the maximum LAI of soybean and spring wheat with RS data, which could also lead to discrepancies between predicted and field LAI. Corn is less sensitive to this problem because the maximum LAI remains stable for a relatively long period. Yield predictions were good for all three crops (RMSE% about 10%). However, two corn yield predictions were in disagreement with observations: fields 2 and 9 in 2008. In field 2, the model underestimated yield by about 2 t ha−1. Yield was also slightly underestimated in fields 5 and 11 (about 0.8 t ha−1) in 2008. Yield measurements for these three corn fields in 2008 (Fields 2, 5 and 11) were done manually, whereas for the other years yields weremeasured witha combineharvester.As the STICSmodel was calibrated using combine harvester measurements [(Jégo](#page-11-0) et [al.,](#page-11-0) [2011),](#page-11-0) which entails a small loss of grains (from 2% to 20% according to [Hanna](#page-11-0) et [al.,](#page-11-0) [2002),](#page-11-0) the model predictions may involve a slight underestimation compared with yield measurements obtained by manual harvesting. In field 9, the actual seeding date was delayed to 10 June, which was 20 days after the recommended date. Even after optimization, the seeding date was still 15 days earlier than the actual date. The late actual seeding date, relative to the optimized date, could explain why the yield was still overestimated (by 1.6 t ha−1) when all RS LAI values were used to re-initialize STICS.

In spite of this poor seeding date prediction, the overall predicted dates were largely improved compared to the default and 2-LAI simulations. The RMSE of prediction was about one week, which is close to the RMSE found by [Ortiz-Monasterio](#page-11-0) [and](#page-11-0) [Lobell](#page-11-0) [(2007)](#page-11-0) for spring wheat seeding date estimation using RS data. In contrast, without using RS LAI, the RMSE was 15 days and with reinitialization of the seeding date using two LAI values, the RMSE was 12 days [(Fig.](#page-9-0) 4, [Table](#page-7-0) 6d). The large improvement in seeding date predictions observed when all LAI values were used confirms that seeding date is a very important parameter to re-initialize in the data assimilation process, as it controls the temporal synchronization between predicted and measured LAI during the fast growth period. The remaining discrepancies can be attributed to LAI estimation errors from RS data (due to interference factors in RS data acquisition or other factors like weed contamination) or poor predictions of emergence date by the crop model. The RMSE% of optimized plant density was equal to the RMSE% of recommended plant density (45%). Plant density mainly affected maximum LAI. However, several factors such as N availability or biotic stresses could affect maximum LAI. This may be why no improvementin the seeding density predictions was noticed after the re-initialization of STICS.

In the case of soil moisture at field capacity, even when all LAI values wereused,the optimized values were only slightly improved compared to values calculated from soil maps using the pedotransfer function (data not shown). The RMSE% between actual and predicted soil moisture at field capacity was 17.2% when the predicted values were calculated from soil maps. This is typical

### 3.4. HighRes simulations

The last set of simulations was done with smaller simulation units, consisting of homogeneous LAI areas, defined using eCognition. This set of simulations was used to test whether model predictions improved when LAI was assimilated based on field subunits defined on LAI-homogeneous zones rather than on field boundaries overlaid with soil texture. First, the actual yield maps showed that yield variability could be high within corn fields, but was lower for spring wheat and soybean [(Table](#page-10-0) 7). However, the within-field variability of yield remained lower than the interannual variability, which was mainly driven by climate variability. The delineation of LAI-homogeneous zones captured most of this variability. [Table](#page-10-0) 7 shows that the number of simulation units was smaller for fields with a lower standard deviation of yield and larger for fields with a higher standard deviation of yield. Yield variability within fields was particularly low (standard deviation equal to or below 5% of average yield) in five fields (corn 2001 F13, spring wheat 2005 F14, and the 3 soybean fields). Fig. 5 and [Table](#page-10-0) 8 show overall good agreement between model-predicted yield and measured yields for the homogeneous zones. The average RMSE% of yield prediction was 14.3%. The predictions were better for corn (RMSE% = 9.9%)than for soybean (RMSE% = 24.1%) and spring wheat (RMSE% = 17.9%). Similar to the actual yield maps, the variability of predicted yields within fields was higher for corn than for spring wheat and soybean. In five fields, the maximum LAI variability (just before the start of senescence) was small(<15%). The standard deviation of the maximum LAI was lower than 0.4 for corn and spring wheat and lower than 0.5 for soybean. However, some fields with low LAI variability (8–9% of average LAI) had high yield variability. Thus, it was not possible to define a threshold above which it is advantageous to delineate LAI-homogeneous zones, for the following reasons: (1) even in fields with low variability, the model yield predictions were good (RMSE% = 18.7%), and (2) there was no link between low within-field maximum LAI variability and low yield variability. The impact of external constraints (climate, disease, etc.) during the senescence stage may explain why the distribution of maximum LAI, even just before the senescence stage, was not always able to capture yield variability within field at harvest. # **4. Conclusion**

Using LAI retrieved from RS data to re-initialize the STICS crop model significantly improved model performance for biomass and yield predictions at the field scale when actual management and soil properties were not available. This study showed that multitemporal visible and near-infrared RS data acquired by different sensors can be used to derive seasonal LAI products with consistency and accuracy for data assimilation into a crop growth model. This is very important, as the integrated use of RS data acquired by different sensors increases the opportunity for cloud-free observation. It is difficult for a single orbital high-resolution sensor to monitor crop growth dynamics, as its revisit cycle is usually long.

Even with only two RS-based LAI estimates for the fast growth period,thus allowing optimization of only one parameter (i.e. seeding date), the predicted biomass and yield values were in good agreement with measurements. This means that if crop type maps were available towards the end of the vegetative growth phase, reliable yield forecasts using different climatic scenarios could be made as soon as the fast growth period ends. When all the LAI (between 4 and 6 values) were used (allowing optimization ofthree parameters: seeding date, seeding density and field capacity), the model predictions were improved and very close to those obtained with actual management and soil properties for all the climatic years tested. The improvements were particularly noticeable for the years characterized by water stress or low GDD, showing the good sensitivity of the whole procedure to climate variation. The optimized seeding dates were consistent with the observed dates and the optimized field capacities were also improved, but only during the water-stress years. There are some limitations to the <span id="page-11-0"></span>tested approach: it does not account for weeds, which can influence the green LAI values extracted from RS, nor does it account for the impact of N and biotic stresses.

Previous studies have shown that accurate LAI, biomass and yield predictions can be obtained for eastern Canada with the STICS model when N is notlimiting. Further work should be done to assess the performance of the STICS crop model in predicting the nitrogen budget under limiting N conditions, in order to include input parameters such as N fertilization and organic nitrogen soil content for optimization purposes and to account for N stress. Biotic stresses are not included in the STICS model yet, and thus the corresponding parameters cannot be optimized. This work paves the way for regional yield predictions in eastern Canada. From an operational point of view, overlaying field boundaries on the soiltexture map may be sufficient to accurately predict yields at the field scale for most regions.
