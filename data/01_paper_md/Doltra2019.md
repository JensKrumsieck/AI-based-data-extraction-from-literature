#### ABSTRACT

Soil fertility building measures should be explored at the short and long-term for an adequate evaluation of their impact on sustaining yields and of its environmental consequences in crop rotations under organic farming. For such a purpose, process-based crop models are potential useful tools to complement and upscale field observations under a range of soil and climatic conditions. Organic rotations differ in soil fertility dynamics in comparison to conventional farming but very few modelling studies have explicitly considered this specific situation. Here, we evaluate the FASSET model to predict the effects of different fertility management options in organic crop rotations on dry matter (DM) and nitrogen (N) yield, and soil N dynamics, including N2O emissions. For that, we used data from seven short and long-term field experiments in different agro-climatic environments in Europe (Norway, Denmark, Poland, Switzerland, Italy and Spain) including climate, soil and management data. Soil fertility building measures covered fertilization type, green manures, cover crops, tillage, crop rotation composition and management (organic or conventional). Model performance was evaluated by comparing observed and simulated values of crop DM and N yield, soil mineral N and nitrous oxide (N2O) emissions using five complementary statistical indices. The model closely reproduced most observed DM and N yield trends and effects of soil fertility building measures in arable crops, particularly in cereals. Contrary, yields of grass-clover, especially N, were generally reproduced with low degree of accuracy. Model performance for simulating soil mineral N depended on site and the availability of soil and management information. Although high uncertainty was associated to the simulation of soil N dynamics, differences of cumulative N2O emissions between fertility building measures were reflected in model outputs. Aspects for modelling improvement include cover crop growth and decomposition, biological N fixation (BNF) or weed and pest soil-crop interactions. It is concluded that FASSET can be successfully used to investigate the impact of fertilization type, green manures, tillage and management (organic or conventional) on crop productivity and to a certain extent on soil N dynamics including soil N2O emissions at different soils and climates in organic farming in Europe.

### 1. Introduction

Management of soil fertility in the organic crop rotations must fit the guidelines described by the Council Regulation (EC) No 834/2007s on organic farming. Soil fertility building measures in organic farming such as tillage system, nitrogen (N) fertilization, use of catch crops or residue management may substantially affect crop yields and soil N cycling. Such measures may help to reduce the yield gap observed between conventional, cropping systems with application of mineral fertilizers and chemical products for crop protection, and organic farming ([Seufert et al., 2012](#page-10-0); [Shah et al., 2017](#page-10-1)) or increase it like in the case of organic conservation tillage systems ([Cooper et al., 2016](#page-9-0)). Growing catch crops, e.g., may reduce the risk of N leaching losses in crop production systems as well as contribute to soil N and carbon (C) pools ([Sapkota et al., 2012)](#page-10-2) increasing the N uptake and the N use efficiency (NUE) of the main crops ([Berntsen et al., 2006](#page-9-1)). Reducing tillage intensity is expected to increase the soil organic matter in the topsoil in comparison to conventional tillage, and thereby reducing soil erosion and improving soil fertility [(Gadermaier et al., 2012](#page-10-3); [Garcia-Franco et al., 2015](#page-10-4); [Cooper et al., 2016](#page-9-0)). However, its effect on crop yields seem to depend on additional factors, such as weather conditions and the implementation of other conservation practices such as residue retention and crop rotation ([Pittelkow et al., 2015](#page-10-5); [Cooper](#page-9-0) [et al., 2016](#page-9-0)). The degree to which management practices sustain optimal inputs from biological N fixation (BNF), manures and crop residues through nutrient re-cycling under organic crop rotations will finally determine the impact of organic agriculture on crops yields and on the environment.

Field experiments are important to monitor effects of varying management practices on crop growth and yield, and on the patterns of soil N dynamics with time. However, field measurements are labour intensive and costly, especially if many factors are investigated and the assessment includes a period of several years. Crop simulation models provide an explicit representation of fundamental bio-physical processes such as crop development and growth, and water and nutrient dynamics in a single crop season or over the seasons [(Wallach et al.,](#page-10-6) [2006](#page-10-6)) and are thus a potential valuable tool to complement and upscale field observations. As such, crop simulations models offer the possibility to explore the short and long-term consequences of agricultural management on crop rotations under a range of soil and climatic conditions.

Several crop simulation models have been developed and applied around the world (e.g. APSIM, [Keating et al., 2003](#page-10-7); CropSyst, [Stöckle](#page-10-8) [et al., 2003](#page-10-8); DSSAT, [Jones et al., 2003](#page-10-9); STICS, [Brisson et al., 2003](#page-9-2)) but only very few simulation studies have been focused on organic crop rotations, particularly performed under Nordic conditions (e.g., [Doltra](#page-10-10) [et al., 2011](#page-10-10); [Nylinder et al., 2011](#page-10-11)). In conventional crop rotations the relative weight of mineralization in N balances decrease with a more intensive use of N fertilizers. Thus, an adequate crop response to mineral N inputs, simpler to be modelled, would be relatively more important than predicting N mineralization for good model performance in these systems. Organic rotations do, however, differ in soil C and N dynamics ([Watson et al., 2002](#page-10-12)) in comparison to conventional farming. Models therefore need to be adjusted and validated to this specific situation. The Farm Assessment Tool (FASSET) [(Olesen et al., 2002](#page-10-13); [Berntsen et al., 2003](#page-9-3)) is a deterministic dynamic model that has been applied in numerous studies under different environmental conditions, including Nordic as well as Mediterranean and continental temperate, and cropping systems (arable crops, grasslands and forage systems) to simulate crop productivity (e.g. [Doltra et al., 2011](#page-10-10); Yin [et al., 2017](#page-10-14)), soil nitrous oxide (N2O) emissions and N leaching ([Berntsen et al.,](#page-9-4) [2005](#page-9-4); [Chatskikh et al., 2005;](#page-9-5) [Doltra et al., 2015](#page-10-15)). This crop model considers seven soil organic matter pools representing added materials, microbial biomass and soil organic matter, and implements functions representing biogeochemical transformations affecting soil C and N dynamics, such as decomposition of organic materials, mineralizationimmobilization or nitrification-denitrification processes ([Petersen et al.,](#page-10-16) [2005](#page-10-16)), that are fundamental for sustaining fertility under organic farming.

In this study, we evaluate the ability of the FASSET model to reproduce the effects of different fertility management options in organic crop rotations on dry matter (DM) and N yield, and soil N dynamics, including N2O emissions on the short and long-term. Data from seven field trials across a range of soil and climatic conditions provide a rich dataset for model testing and evaluation representing major regions in Europe.

#### 2. Materials and methods

### 2.1. Experimental data

Datasets from organic crop rotations covering short and long-term field experiments in different agro-climatic environments in Europe were used for testing and adjusting the FASSET model. The selection included seven datasets from six countries (Norway, Denmark, Poland, Switzerland, Italy and Spain), representing different climates, soils and fertility building measures. The fertility building measures include fertilization type, green manures, cover crops, tillage, crop rotation composition and management (organic, integrated or conventional). Collected experiments cover a variety of cropping systems and crop rotations, comprising field data and measures such as crop dry matter and N yield, soil mineral N and soil N2O emissions. Not all measurements were, however, available in all the experiments, with lesser data for soil N dynamics than for crop productivity in the study datasets. Provided data on soil mineral N included Foulum (Denmark), Frick (Switzerland), Osiny (Poland) and Værnes (Norway) experiments and data on N2O fluxes were taken at Foulum and Frick. ### 2.2. FASSET model

The FASSET model ([www.fasset.dk)](http://www.fasset.dk) is a whole-farm model that simulates crop growth and soil C and N dynamics in the soil-crop-atmosphere system, including greenhouse gas fluxes, at a daily time scale. A detailed description of the crop model can be found in [Olesen et al.](#page-10-13) [(2002)](#page-10-13) and [Berntsen et al. (2003)](#page-9-3). All cropping systems were simulated with the latest version 2.5 of the FASSET model. Input data necessary to run the model comprised climate (at least daily values of maximum and minimum temperatures, precipitation and solar radiation), data related to soil physical-chemical characteristics (such as soil texture, bulk density and organic matter) and management information (sowing, harvesting, fertilization and tillage) of the aforementioned experiments.

Soil model initialization was done by simulating 2–4 years prior to the start of the treatment using the available management information at the plot level and historical weather data. When such historical management data were not available, the crop sequence under study was repeated in the simulation in order to initialize the model (i. e. sites in Spain). The influence of the level of available information for FASSET initialization of soil organic matter pools has been highlighted in other modelling studies ([Doltra et al., 2015](#page-10-15)), and with other dynamic crop models (e.g. [Luo et al., 2014](#page-10-25)).

In a first step, crop phenology parameters of the model were adjusted to reproduce the average crop development within a given site (based on observations, expert knowledge or the literature). With adjusted crop phenology parameters, simulated crop yield, biomass DM and N were evaluated. If necessary, crop parameters related to radiation use efficiency, canopy development and grain N storage were also adjusted in the model [(Table 2](#page-3-0)). Model behaviour was evaluated by comparing observed and simulated values of crop DM and N yield, soil mineral N and N2O fluxes.

#### 2.4. Statistical analysis

Five statistical indices were calculated to explore and assess model ability to simulate both crop productivity and soil N variables. First, the coefficient of determination r 2 , which is an indicator of the closeness of the relationship between model predictions and observations, was used:

$$r^2 = \frac{\left(\sum_{l} (\mathbf{M}_l - \overset{\cdot}{\mathbf{M}})(\mathbf{O}_l - \overset{\cdot}{\mathbf{O}})\right)^2}{\sum (\mathbf{M}_l - \overset{\cdot}{\mathbf{M}})^2 \sum (\mathbf{O}_l - \overset{\cdot}{\mathbf{O}})^2}$$

where Oi and Mi are the observed and modelled values; *O* ¯ and *M* ¯ are the means of all the observed and modelled values. This parameter ranges from 0 to 1, with values close to 1 indicating that the model is highly precise, while the lowest precision level is represented by 0.

Second, the index of agreement (d), which is a dimensionless statistical index varying between 0 and 1. It reflects the relative size of the differences between model simulations and observations ([Willmott,](#page-10-26) [1982](#page-10-26)):

$$\mathbf{d} = \mathbf{1} - \frac{\sum_{l}^{n} \left( \mathbf{O}_{l} - \mathbf{M}_{l} \right)^{2}}{\sum_{l}^{n} \left( l O_{l}^{\prime} l - l \mathbf{M}^{\prime} l \right)^{2}}$$

where *ǀO*′ *ǀi* and *ǀM*′ *ǀ i* are the differences with respect to the average observation, of the observed and model simulated values, respectively.

Third, the root mean square error (RMSE), which is a measure of the differences between observations and predictions, was calculated following the equation proposed by [Willmott (1982)](#page-10-26):

$$\text{RMSE} = \sqrt{\frac{\sum_{l}^{n} (O_l - M_l)^2}{n}}$$

where n is the number of observations. The lower the RMSE, the better the simulated values agree with the observations.

Fourth, the mean bias error (MBE) shows the systematic deviations. A negative MBE is an indication of model underestimation and a positive value indicates overestimation. This statistical index was calculated by the equation of [Willmott (1982)](#page-10-26):

$$\text{MBE} = \frac{\sum_{l}^{n} \left( \mathbf{M}_{l} - \mathbf{O}_{l} \right)}{\mathbf{n}}$$

Finally, the model efficiency (EF) was calculated following [Nash and](#page-10-27)

### Sutcliff[e (1970)](#page-10-27):

$$\text{EF} = 1 - \frac{\sum (\mathbf{M}_{\parallel} - \mathbf{O}_{l})^{2}}{\sum (\mathbf{O}_{l} - \mathbf{O})^{2}}$$

EF ranges from −1 to 1, with an efficiency of 1 indicating the perfect match of the simulations to the observations, while values lower than zero indicate that the mean of the observations is a better predictor than the simulated value.

### 3. Results

### 3.1. Model performance

The ability of the FASSET model to simulate DM and N yield is shown in [Table 3](#page-4-0) pooling all experimental sites. For the whole set of environments, experiments and fertility building measures, a better performance of model simulations was found for arable crops than for grass-clover (managed such as ley or rotational grassland), and for DM than for N, as it is reflected in all the statistical indexes. Among crops, the model showed a better ability to simulate growth of cereals, and particularly for wheat (data not shown), which is the crop with greatest presence in this study (80 observations of DM and 25 observations of N). Positive EF values and high d coefficients are indicative of satisfactory model simulations of DM yield for all arable crops and grassclover and of N yield for cereals. While there is a slight systematic underestimation of DM and N yield for arable crops, FASSET highly overestimated grass-clover production.

At Villaviciosa, maize yield was higher after the shorter duration of grassland, which was also reflected in the simulations for maize DM but not for N.

The effect of the management of green manures at Værnes was reproduced by the model, both on DM and N yield. Mulching grass clover lead to an increase in spring barley DM and N yield compared to removal of grass-clover herbage.

The response of crop DM and N yield to the application of organic manure or mineral fertilizers compared with unfertilized treatments were overall well replicated in model simulations as found previously at different sites in Denmark ([Doltra et al., 2011](#page-10-10)), as well as in other modelling studies under organic or conventional cropping systems (e.g., [MacCarthy et al., 2009;](#page-10-30) [David et al., 2004;](#page-9-11) [Doltra and Muñoz, 2010)](#page-10-31). Still, in most but not all cases, modelled yields tended to be underestimated in the mineral N treatments. However, the effects of different types of manure could not be well distinguished in the simulations. Nutrient release with time varies with manure source but this variation was probably not well reproduced by FASSET. Decomposition dynamics of organic products in the field is an aspect that would require further attention in the model. This, however, would involve measurements that are difficult to obtain.

The FASSET model was, in general, able to capture the effect of varying tillage systems on winter wheat at Frick. Yet, the reduced tillage system included a pea crop used as green manure before the sowing of maize in 2008 in contrast to ploughing ([Krauss et al., 2010](#page-10-32)). Although the model captured an increase in soil N due to the pea incorporation of the same magnitude as the N present in the pea intercrop (data not shown), it was unable to reproduce its substantial effect on maize DM and N yield after pea incorporation in this treatment. Thus, model performance for simulating tillage system effects finally does not only depends on a proper simulation of the tillage operations, but it also heavily relies on the impact of other interacting factors, such as rates of decomposition of plant and root residues and subsequent plant N availability.

However, this was improved for cumulative grass-clover yields at annual scale, enabling the model to simulate the response of silage maize yield to the duration of previous grass-clover (winter season, one year or two years).

### 4.2. Modelling fertility measures effects on soil mineral N and N2O emissions

While soil mineral N was successfully simulated at Foulum and Værnes, at Osiny lack of available measured input data for total soil N or C/N ratio and uncertainties related to the depth of soil profile affected the quality of soil mineral N simulations. The deviations between simulated and measured soil mineral N at Frick were mainly produced during the grass-clover period, when soil mineral N is underestimated in this clayey soil, probably due to high uncertainty in the simulation of grass-clover biomass and N, as earlier stated. Besides being aware of soil spatial heterogeneity, leading to microenvironments that affect soil conditions and local N chemical transformations, it is crucial that sufficient detailed information of the recent crop and soil management is known for modelling soil, but that, often, it is not available ([Doltra](#page-10-15) [et al., 2015](#page-10-15)).

Although the seasonal dynamics of soil temperature and WFPS was well reproduced by the model, this does not avoid high uncertainty associated to the simulation of daily soil mineral N and N2O flux dynamics. This is most evident in days when soil operations such as tillage or incorporation of crop residues and manures, take places as seen in treatments at Frick and Foulum. This problem has also been reported in other studies (e.g., [Abdalla et al., 2014](#page-9-12)), and it reflects the complexity and the limitations for representing the interactions between factors governing the nitrification-denitrification processes with the current implemented functions in biogeochemical models. However, these peaks can represent a significant part of the cumulative seasonal N2O emissions [(Molodovskaya et al., 2012)](#page-10-34), thus causing a substantial deviation with respect to the simulated values as found here and in previous studies (e.g. [Doltra et al., 2015](#page-10-15)). These peaks of N2O fluxes are triggered by environmental conditions ([Molodovskaya et al., 2012;](#page-10-34) [Doltra et al., 2015;](#page-10-15) [Westphal et al., 2018)](#page-10-35), usually well captured in modelling, in combination with the applications of manures, crop residues or tillage. The interaction of the many different processes conducting to N2O fluxes is hard to predict and may be the reason for uneven results. For instance, some studies reported a decrease in cumulative N2O emissions under reduced tillage (e.g. [Chatskikh et al.,](#page-9-13) [2008](#page-9-13); [Mutegi et al., 2010](#page-10-36)) while others observed the opposite, an increase in N2O emissions by reducing tillage intensity [(Ball et al., 1999](#page-9-14)). [Pugesgaard et al. (2017)](#page-10-37) reported that mineralization of crop residues may be the main driver. The dataset of Frick used in this study beyond revealed that increasing topsoil fertility (more SOC and microbial biomass) in the long-term by a tillage system change or additional application of solid manures, bears the risk of high N2O flux peaks after tillage operations when microbes are stimulated ([Krauss et al., 2017](#page-10-21)). Those hot moments caused the treatment differences in 2014 ([Table 6)](#page-8-0).

Cover crops accumulate N in tops and roots, which after soil incorporation and decomposition can lead to substantial N2O emissions ([Li et al., 2015)](#page-10-38), in particular if cover crops have high N concentrations, which happens more frequently with inclusion of legumes in the cover crops ([Basche et al., 2014)](#page-9-15). The simulated effects of legume-based cover crops on N2O emissions were higher than observed at Foulum, although the sense of the impact was the same ([Table 6](#page-8-0)). The effects of incorporation of cover crops on N2O emissions can be short-lived, and therefore manual measurements on a weekly basis, as done in the experiment, may not always capture the full effects of these events. Also, these effects interact with the environmental conditions, e.g. soil water filled pore space ([Li et al., 2016](#page-10-39)).

In contrast to single N2O fluxes, when differences of cumulative seasonal or annual N2O emissions between fertility building measures and years were noticeable (i.e. above 0.38 kg N2O-N ha−1 in this study), they were overall captured with FASSET. Therefore, in these situations it could be possible to predict the impact of management practice changes on cumulative N2O emissions in organic rotations with this model.

#### 4.3. Modelling organic crop rotations

There are fewer simulation studies carried out under organic farming conditions compared to conventional arable cropping systems ([Yin et al., 2017)](#page-10-14). Organic crop rotations require complex fertility strategies that are not necessary in conventional farming ([Gattinger](#page-10-40) [et al., 2012;](#page-10-40) [Lorenz and Lal, 2016)](#page-10-41). A proper representation of BNF and nutrients release from organic materials in soils is necessary in crop models for a successful simulation of cropping systems in organic farming. The simulation results in this study indicate that FASSET provides a trade-off between complexity of modelling C and N transformations and model parameterization that is adequate for the estimate of crop yields and, to a lesser degree, soil mineral N dynamics and cumulative N2O emissions. However, improvement of time resolution in modelling soil N2O fluxes or decomposition rates of organic N sources would be necessary for a better assessment of soil fertility measures. Moreover, no comprehensive process-based model to simulate crop rotations has been specifically developed for organic farming, thus the implementation of mineralization and turnover algorithms in other models are often from too simple to non-existing ([Kollas et al.,](#page-10-29) [2015](#page-10-29); [Yin et al., 2017](#page-10-14)).

Weeds, pests and diseases are other factors with commonly much higher impact on crop performance in organic than in conventional farming. They are presently not considered in most of process-based models including FASSET. [David et al. (2005)](#page-10-42) argued that predicting effects of weeds, pests and diseases on crop yields was necessary for selecting strategies in organic winter wheat based on modelling exercises. To some extent all these factors have probably contributed to the uncertainty in the simulated effects of soil fertility building measures across all cropping systems investigated in this study. Since most of crop rotation modelling studies have been focused on conventional farming (e.g., [Kollas et al., 2015](#page-10-29); [Yin et al., 2017](#page-10-14)), only little attention has been paid to improve their representation in crop models.

Hence, remaining challenges in modelling organic arable crop rotations and improvement of FASSET model are the improvement and validation of BNF and N mineralization-immobilization algorithms under different environments, and the consideration of weeds and other biotic stresses occurring in the cropping system.

### 4.4. Modelling across environments and cropping systems and implications for modelling long-term scenarios

The results found here have shown that FASSET is applicable to investigate at long-term soil fertility scenarios relevant in organic farming, although this is circumscribed to those sites and fertility building measures where the observed effects on a given productive and/or environmental variable could be reproduced with the model. In most cases, simulated and observed results matched well at sites where crops and factors for which the model was originally developed were included: i.e, cool temperate and Nordic environments (Foulum and Værnes), grain cereals, fertilization and cover crops treatments. Moreover, as shown in other studies (e.g [Huth et al., 2010](#page-10-43); [Doltra et al.,](#page-10-15) [2015](#page-10-15)) it is important to have sufficiently detailed information of the cropping system, current and historical management and of the environment to simulate soil fertility building measure effects on crop yield and seasonal soil and plant N dynamics well. This became evident when modelling results from Frick, with detailed information and temperate-oceanic climatic conditions, and from Osiny with lacking soil and manure N information and continental conditions. On the other hand, differences in FASSET performance obtained between crop types indicate that the composition and proportion of crops within the rotation should be considered in the definition of the long-term scenarios for modelling.

As a general rule, scenarios based on highly uncertain model predictions according to the results reported here should be disregarded. However, in spite of the observed higher uncertainty that legumes and grass-clover periods add to the N dynamics during parts of the season, e.g. at Frick, they are fundamental in building fertility and sustain yields in organic farming. Grass-clover models have been specifically developed for grasslands and swards (e.g. [Topp and Doyle, 1996](#page-10-44); [Lazzarotto et al., 2009](#page-10-45)) in Europe, but not yet for arable cropping systems. Thus, we identified that it is fundamental to improve the simulation of grass-clover growth and BNF for the case of organically managed cropping systems. This is a challenge which needs field work and simultaneous model development as part of future organic farming research.

#### 5. Conclusions

Overall, the results of the model testing allowed to conclude that FASSET can be used to investigate scenarios based on most relevant soil fertility building measures in organic arable systems. At different soils and climates in Europe, it was successfully used to investigate the impact of fertilization type, including grass-clover green manure, tillage and management (organic or conventional) on crop productivity and to a certain extent on soil N dynamics including soil N2O emissions. However, progress in modelling key crops and processes enhancing soil fertility and sustaining cropping systems under organic farming is still essential. This includes cover crops DM and N yields, BNF in legume crops and in grass-clover, above and belowground residues mineralization, and weed and pest soil-crop interactions. The impact of soil processes contributing to sustain crop growth and yield differs between organic and conventional crop management. Therefore, there is the need of more experimental studies in organic farming enabling the improvement of simulation approaches in process-based crop models, particularly for grass-clover as a fertility building measure in rotations dominated by arable crops.
