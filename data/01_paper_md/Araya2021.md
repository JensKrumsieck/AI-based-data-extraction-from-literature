# Using crop simulation model to evaluate influence of water management practices and multiple cropping systems on crop yields: A case study for Ethiopian highlands

## ABSTRACT

Low soil fertility, soil water stress and deteriorating natural resources are among the causal factors to low crop productivity in Africa. In this study, impact of cropping systems and water management practices on yield of different crops were simulated using the Decision Support System for Agrotechnology Transfer-Cropping System Model. The treatments included continuous cropping (monoculture) of three different crops [wheat, WT; barley, BA; chickpea, CP]; and rotation systems (WT-BA, and WT-BA-CP). Crops were treated with three different water management practices (a) rainfed; (b) tied ridging; and (c) irrigation. Five years (2013− 2017) average actual yields were obtained and average short-term (2013− 2017) and long-term (1984− 2019) yield gaps, relative yields and yield changes were calculated. The yield gap (Yg) [the difference between actual (Ya) and simulated rainfed yield (Yw)] for wheat, barley and chickpea were 0.0 to 0.6, 0.1 to 1.7 and 0.2 to 2.1 Mg/ha, respectively. The large Yg value for chickpea was mainly attributed to the water stress. Whereas barley and wheat yield were less affected by the water stress when compared to chickpea. The rotation WT-BA-CP was more responsive to water management than WT-BA. Tied ridging or irrigations can be considered as an option for improving yield in water scarce areas. Chickpea fixed substantial amount of nitrogen (90–246 kg N/ha) in the soil, which could result in yield benefits and reduced use of inorganic nitrogen. Overall, the rotation WT-BA-CP may be a better option than WT-BA or continuous cropping as it can provide multiple benefits via enhanced crop diversity, improved soil fertility and more resilience to pest and diseases. This simulation case study and discussion of results showed the importance of integration of crop rotations with soil and water conservation practices for improving overall yields and contributions to ecological intensification.

# **1. Introduction**

Livelihoods of population in sub-Saharan Africa depends mainly on agriculture. Several countries in sub-Saharan Africa are facing food deficits and low crop productivity due to land degradation, poor soil fertility and changing climate [(Dile et al., 2013;](#page-10-0) [Silungwe et al., 2018;](#page-11-0) [Stewart et al., 2020](#page-11-0)). Ethiopia is one of the sub-Saharan African country that suffer from frequent food insecurity partly due to low crop yield attributed to multiple abiotic and biotic factors such as prolonged drought, water scarcity, land degradation, poor soil fertility and pests and diseases ([Stroosnijder, 2009](#page-11-0); [Araya and Stroosnijder, 2010;](#page-10-0) [Tadele,](#page-11-0)  [2017)](#page-11-0). In addition, continuous cropping (monoculture) and decline in fallow land are major contributors to low crop productivity [(Teklewold](#page-11-0) [et al., 2013)](#page-11-0) and large yield gaps in cereal and legume crops. In Ethiopia, the actual yield under current conditions is much lower than the attainable or potential yield ([Taffesse et al., 2011](#page-11-0)). Over the past decades, increase in total cereal production in Ethiopia was attributed to increased cultivable land ([Taffesse et al., 2011)](#page-11-0). Due to the limited scope in expanding the arable land and fast deteriorating natural resources ([Stroosnijder, 2009; Tadele, 2017; Vanlauwe et al., 2019)](#page-11-0), focus should be on sustainable intensification, i.e. increasing crop productivity per unit land area per unit time from existing farm land and without negative impacts on the environment ([Pretty and Bharucha, 2014;](#page-10-0) [Schut](#page-10-0)  [et al., 2016;](#page-10-0) [Adhikari et al., 2018](#page-9-0)). Recent review by [Pretty et al. (2018)](#page-10-0)  estimated significant progress towards sustainable intensification on 163 million farms and 453 million ha, indicating that such systems could

Agroecological intensification is one of the key pillars of sustainable intensification, others being genetic intensification and socio-economic intensification. Agroecological intensification involves integration of ecological principles, resource use efficiency and biodiversity management (taking into account soil health, nutrient cycling, diversification, beneficial interactions of multiple species, water availability and natural resource management) with the goal of enhancing ecosystem services while increasing productivity and profitability with reduced reliance on external inputs ([Garbach et al., 2016](#page-10-0)). There is a growing interest in implementation of agroecological intensification programs ([Tadesse](#page-11-0)  [et al., 2015;](#page-11-0) [Gebremariam, 2015](#page-10-0); [Molina and Casado, 2017;](#page-10-0) [Balehegn](#page-10-0)  [et al., 2019)](#page-10-0). Implementation of agroecological restoration programs based on principles of conservation agriculture, rainwater conservation, crop diversification are reported to enhance soil health and improve soil physical, biological and chemical properties, and increase nutrient use efficiency of a cropping system and reduces pressure of weeds ([Siddique](#page-11-0)  [et al., 2012;](#page-11-0) [Garbach et al., 2016](#page-10-0); [Vanlauwe et al., 2019)](#page-11-0).

As component of agroecological intensification, soil and water conservation practices and crop diversification are under investigation. Soil and rainwater conservation practices reduce runoff, enhance infiltration, decrease soil erosion, and increase soil water availability, all contributing to the yield improvement ([McHugh et al., 2007](#page-10-0); [Stroos](#page-11-0)[nijder, 2009;](#page-11-0) [Temesgen et al., 2009;](#page-11-0) [Araya and Stroosnijder, 2010](#page-10-0); [Biazin and Stroosnijder, 2012; Ngetich et al., 2014](#page-10-0); [Okeyo et al., 2014)](#page-10-0). Soil and water conservation technologies such as tied ridging ([Araya and](#page-10-0)  [Stroosnijder, 2010](#page-10-0)) and cereal-legume rotations in combination with improved crop cultivars are effective agroecological intensification practices to enhance yield and food security ([Teklewold et al., 2013](#page-11-0); [Silungwe et al., 2018](#page-11-0)). Tied ridging increased soil water storage and water use efficiency relative to subsoiling-ripping practices ([Miriti et al.,](#page-10-0)  [2012;](#page-10-0) [Okeyo et al., 2014](#page-10-0)). [Silungwe et al. (2018)](#page-11-0) evaluated several strategies for reducing cereal production risks under rainfed conditions (smallholder systems), and tied ridging was found to be suitable and effective technology for improving soil water availability and yield for smallholder farming systems in Africa. [Kiboi et al. (2017)](#page-10-0) indicated the importance of tied ridging for stabilizing maize yield. Similarly, [Traore](#page-11-0) [et al. (2020)](#page-11-0) highlighted the importance of water conservation methods and crop rotations for improving cropping system productivity.

The impacts of integration of crop diversification (crop rotations system with legumes) and water management practices (rainfed, tied ridging and irrigation) on yield have not been tested across multiple seasons and locations. In addition, there have been limited information on yield performances of common crop rotations across multiple years in Ethiopia to capture seasonal variability. Conventional field experiments to document such information are difficult, costly and time consuming. Crop models are useful tools to analyze crops production systems and help in investigating management scenarios across time and space ([Jones et al., 2003](#page-10-0); [Araya et al., 2020;](#page-10-0) [Jha et al., 2018](#page-10-0)). Crop models have the capability to simulate yield by integrating crop characteristics, climatic conditions, soils and management practices under various growing conditions ([Jones et al., 2003;](#page-10-0) [Araya et al., 2020)](#page-10-0). [Silungwe](#page-11-0) [et al. (2018)](#page-11-0) reported that crop models are useful to evaluate crop failure risks, identify, simulate and expand the implementations of best management practices from local to regional level. These practices include options for agroecological intensification.

In this study, we used the Decision Support System for Agrotechnology Transfer-Cropping System Model (DSSAT) model v.4.7 ([Hoogenboom et al., 2017)](#page-10-0) and analyzed the impacts of continuous cropping, crop rotation system, irrigation and tied-ridging practices on yield performance of the major cereal crops grown in the Ethiopian highlands as a case study considering long-term climatic conditions. The objectives of research were to (i) identify suitable cropping system in two regions (represented by Adigudom and Kulumsa sites) in Ethiopia as a case study for African highlands; and (ii) simulate the impact of water management practices on crop performance, determine yield gaps and

relative yields for continuous and crop rotation systems.

## **2. Materials and methods**

## *2.1. Experimental study sites*

Barley experiment was conducted during the cropping season in 2004, 2008, 2009 and 2010 at Mekelle and in 2010 at Maychew site, in the northern Ethiopia. Mekelle is located at latitude of 13◦ 3′ N and longitude of 39◦ 32′ E with an elevation of 2210 m above sea level. The average short-term (2004–2010) measured climate data that include daily rainfall, minimum and maximum temperature and solar radiation for the experimental sites was obtained from the Endayesus experimental site weather station. The seasonal (Jul to Nov) minimum and maximum temperature for Mekelle were 13 and 26 ◦C, respectively. The seasonal and annual rainfall is approximately 513 and 649 mm, respectively. The soil was moderately friable, well drained (moderate infiltration rate) clay loam with a depth of 0.6 m (Table S1). The effective root zone, where most of the nutrient and soil water absorption occurs ([Raes, 2002](#page-10-0)), was approximately 0.6 m.

The second experimental site, Maychew, is located at latitude of 12◦ 47′ and longitude of 39◦ 32′ E with an elevation of 2400 m above sea level. Like for Mekelle, the short-term (2004–2010) measured climate data that include minimum and maximum temperature, solar radiation and daily rainfall was obtained from the Maychew weather station. The average daily minimum and maximum temperature for the cropping season (Jul to Nov) was 10.7 ◦C and 22 ◦C, respectively with seasonal (Jul to Nov) and annual rainfall sum of 475 and 675 mm, respectively. The soil at Maychew was friable, well-drained loam with a depth of 0.75 m (Table S1).

The treatments at both Mekelle and Maychew were tied ridging (a type of soil water conservation) and planting date. Detail description of the tied ridging practice is available in prior study ([Araya and](#page-10-0)  [Stroosnjider, 2010](#page-10-0)). The planting date treatments were barley planting on July 4 (early), July 12 (normal) and July 22 (late) based on the local practice. The control treatment in this case is the practice of growing barley under rainfed condition, which is practiced by majority of farmers (producers) in the study site. All treatments were replicated three times and arranged in randomized complete block designed. The experimental season data in 2010 based on early and normal planting at both Mekelle and Maychew sites were used for barley model calibration and evaluation, respectively. The experimental seasons 2004, 2008, 2009 and 2010 at Mekelle and 2010 at Maychew sites were used for modeling soil water with tied ridging in relation to soil water storage in the control treatment at the study sites.

#### *2.2. Soil water in tied ridging and model setup*

Profile soil water storage measurements were taken from barley field (at 15–20 cm interval up to 60 cm) during the growing seasons in 2004, 2008, 2009 and 2010 at Mekelle and in 2010 at Maychew site ([Araya](#page-10-0)  [and Stroosnijder, 2010;](#page-10-0) [Tezera, 2011)](#page-11-0). The profile soil water measurement was carried out from time of planting/seed establishment to maturity using gravimetric method ([Araya and Stroosnijder, 2010](#page-10-0)). Most of the soil water measurement were carried out every 10 days interval during the growing season. However, there were few exceptional occasions where soil water measurements were carried out at 7 and 14-days interval due to labor constraints and unfavorable weather factors. However, an overall of 10-daily average is assumed to represent the measured result. Since the soil types in 2010 at Mekelle was slightly different from 2004, 2008 and 2009, the relationship between 10-daily profile soil water in tied ridge and the control treatment in 2010 was presented and demonstrated based on two options:

- (i) The 2010 Mekelle experiment was combined with similar barley tied ridging experiment during the same climatic season in 2010
at Maychew site to generate relationships between soil water in tied ridging and the corresponding periodically measured profile soil water for the control treatment. In addition, the periodically measured profile soil water in tied ridging during the cropping season in 2010 at both sites was regressed against the periodic rainfall sum of the respective sites.

- (ii) All barley experiment (2004, 2008, 2009, and 2010) combined to generate relationships between the periodic (10-daily) profile soil water in tied ridging against the corresponding measured soil water in the control treatment. In addition, the periodically measured soil water across the seasons (2004, 2008, 2009, and 2010) were regressed against the corresponding periodically received rainfall sum regardless of difference in climatic season and soil type. Following linear regressions between the periodic profile soil water in the tied ridging treatment and the control, better preliminary result was obtained for simulating tied ridging experiment than the regressions between the periodic profile soil water in the tied ridging against the corresponding periodic rainfall sum across the seasons. Finally, soil water increased due to presence of tied ridging relative to the control was then quantified and modeled.
## *2.3. Model calibration and validation*

The CSCER047 – barley model in DSSAT was adjusted for soil and climate conditions, and management practices implemented at Mekelle experiment during the cropping season in 2010. At Mekelle sites, the barley crop, which was treated with soil water conservation (tied ridging) and the control (without tied ridging) were planted on July 4 and July 12. The treatments were replicated three times in randomized complete block design. Accordingly, the model was set to plant on July 4 and 12 with a plant population of 100 plants/m2 . The model was set according to the initial conditions and agronomic management of the experimental site. For example, the initial soil water and nitrogen (at time of planting) was adjusted to 55 % of the maximum and 5 kg/ha, respectively, based on information at the experimental site. In addition, the model was set with a nitrogen fertilizer application of 64 kg N/ha applied in two split (half at time of planting and the other half a month after planting).

The increase in profile soil water with tied ridging was quantified and represented in the model by setting irrigation as obtained from the statistical model Eqs. (2) and (3). Accordingly, the linear regression equations presented in Eqs. (3) and (4) showed an increase in 10-daily profile soil water throughout the growing season (planting o maturity) by the range of 23–27 mm over the control (without tied ridging). Therefore, an average irrigation of 25 mm every 10-days from planting to maturity was assumed to represent the tied ridge with R2 of 0.7 (Table S3).

Following calibration of the soil water in treatment with the tied ridging, and entry of climate, soil and management data, the model was calibrated for days to flowering and maturity, yield and biomass both for tied ridging and control treatment. The genetic coefficient in DSSAT4.7 database var# IB0101 ecotype "SY0001" was slightly adjusted to reproduce the observed days to flowering and maturity, measured biomass and yield at the Mekelle experimental site (Table S2).

The model was validated using dataset obtained in another independent experimental site (Maychew) for ted ridging and control treatments during the cropping season in 2010. The model was set to initial soil water (at time of planting) of 55 % (relative to maximum limit) and the initial soil nitrogen was set to 5 kg/ha (undocumented data). In addition, all other soil, crop, climate and management practice were adjusted according the Maychew experimental site. Like for Mekelle site, the nitrogen fertilizer rate was set to 64 kg N/ha. The application timing was half at time of planting and the other half at 30 days after planting. The effective rooting depth and tied ridging was set in the model as implemented in the calibration dataset. The barley

genetic coefficient obtained through the calibration process was then used for simulating the biomass, yield, days to flowering and maturity for the tied ridging and the control treatment. Goodness of fit test was carried out based on statistical techniques such as index of agreement (I) ([Willmott, 1982)](#page-11-0), percent of deviation, normalized mean square of error (NMSE) and R2, were used for evaluating the model. Finally, the genetic coefficient was used for simulating scenarios in this study.

## *2.4. Simulation study sites*

The simulation study was carried out at two selected sites namely: Adigudom (13◦ 15′ E and 39◦ 30′ N, with an elevation of 2068 m a.s.l.) and Kulumsa (8◦ 00′ to 8◦ 02′ N and 39◦ 07′ to 39◦ 10′ E, with an elevation of 1980− 2230 m a.s.l.) located in Tigray and Oromia regional states of Ethiopia, respectively ([Fig. 1](#page-3-0)). The two sites were selected as a case study representing highlands of Africa due to the type of crops grown and cropping systems, agroecology and farming practices. The dominant soil texture at Kulumsa site is Clay ([Sertsu et al., 2003)](#page-11-0) whereas the dominant soils in Adigudom site are clay and sandy clay loam. The soil characteristics were obtained from [Sertsu et al. (2003)](#page-11-0); [Berhanu et al. (2013](#page-10-0)), and other non-peer reviewed reports. The field capacity for clay and sandy clay loam soils were 0.50− 0.54, 0.40− 0.46, and 0.25− 0.29 mm/mm, respectively, whereas, the wilting points for the corresponding soils were 0.29− 0.31, 0.16− 0.19, and 0.15− 0.18 mm/mm, respectively. Agroecologically, both Adigudom and Kulumsa are categorized locally as "*Woina Dega*" ([Gorfu and Ahmed, 2011)](#page-10-0) where vast majorities of cereals and pulses grow. Long-term (1984–2019) meteorological data that include daily minimum and maximum temperature, and solar radiation data was obtained from the National Aeronautics and Space Administration's (NASA) Prediction of Worldwide Energy Resource (POWER).While the daily rainfall was extracted from the Climate Hazards Group Infrared Precipitation with Stations (CHIRPS) ([Funk et al., 2014](#page-10-0), [2015;](#page-10-0) [Dinku et al., 2018)](#page-10-0). The long-term mean annual daily maximum temperature (T), minimum T, and annual total rainfall for Adigudom site were 26.7 ◦C, 13.8 ◦C and 643 mm, respectively. Similarly, the corresponding values for Kulumsa site were 23.1 ºC, 10.0 ◦C and 820 mm, respectively.

# *2.5. Scenarios simulations*

In this simulation study, three crops (wheat, WT; Barley, BA; and chickpea, CP) were used in different systems: continuous cropping (wheat–wheat; barley–barely; and chickpea–chickpea) and crop rotations (wheat–barley; wheat–barley–chickpea). The wheat cultivar that was calibrated in DSSAT in prior study presented in [Araya et al. (2019)](#page-10-0)  was used for this simulation study (Table S4). As the barley calibrated in this study is not a dominant one in the region ("Barley – 2") (Table S4), the simulation was merged and averaged with another dominant barley [calibrated in prior study ("Barley – 1") (Table S4)]. The genetic coefficient for the chickpea cultivar "Arartie" as validated in [Urgaya (2016)](#page-11-0)  (available in DSSAT database (var # IB0012, Ecotype code: "KAK2 KABULE") was used for simulating the scenarios in this study.

The crops were planted each season at both simulation study sites on July 20 at a planting density of 250 plants/m2 and planting depth of 3 cm based on local practice. Nitrogen fertilizer was applied for the crops each season at a rate of 64 kg N/ha in two split (half at the time of planting and the other half 30 days after planting). Whereas chickpea was planted on July 1 with no N fertilizer at both locations based on the local practice. The crops/cropping systems were grown under three soil water management scenarios (i) rainfed, (ii) soil water conservation (tied ridging) and, (iii) irrigation (applying four irrigation every 10-days with a depth of 25 mm from time of heading to late grain filling. The period (Sep. 1–30), correspond to heading to late grain filling for wheat and barley while early grain filling to late grain filling for chickpea. The most benefit of tied ridging or irrigation occurs late in the season after flowering when crops are sensitive to water stress, which often leads to

#### *2.6. Evaluating crop performance*

The model was set with similar fertilizer application and other management practices, which are practiced in both Adigudom and Kulumsa locations as presented in sections above. The yield and biomass of all crops and cropping system were simulated for the various treatments. Performance of crops was evaluated by comparing actual yield (Ya) against the water limited yield (Yw), (yield simulated under rainfed condition) ([van Wart et al., 2013)](#page-11-0), yield in tied ridging (YTR); and yield under four irrigations (Yi4). The Ya is defined as the latest five-years average yield as reported by Ethiopian Central Statistical Agency for the crops around the study sites. Long-term (1984− 2019) and short-term (2013–2017) Yw, YTR and Yi4 (for all crops and cropping systems) was simulated and compared with the Ya. Relative yield (Ry) was also calculated for all crops and cropping systems as the ratio of Ya to Yw or YTR or Yi4 multiplied by 100. Yield gap (Yg) was used as a criteria for evaluating the performance of crops and cropping systems scenarios. Since majority of producers grow crops under rainfed condition, the yield gap in our study was estimated as the difference between the Ya and Yw as presented by [Hochman et al. (2016).](#page-10-0) In addition, we also extended the application of Yg as the difference between the YTR and Ya. The Yg for irrigated crop rotation systems was estimated as a difference between average combined crop rotation's Yi4 and Ya. Therefore, for cropping system performance evaluation purposes, long-term yields of individual crops per each rotation systems (W-BA and WT-BA-CP) were mixed and averaged as one crop (here after aggregated yield). Each rotation systems were compared with each other with in the same site and against the other site in terms of Yw, Ry, Yg and percent yield change from Ya. The aggregated Yg was estimated as Yw, YTR, or Yi4 minus Ya.

Since the actual yield for each crop at the Adigudom site (as obtained from Central Statistical Agency in Ethiopia) are obtained from combinations of clay and sandy clay loam soils, the simulated average yield from two soils was combined and averaged for this study (not from single soil). Whereas the actual yield for Kulumsa study site was obtained from one dominant soil, clay ([Sertsu et al., 2003)](#page-11-0). The other difference between sites (Adigudom and Kulumsa) was the climate (such as temperature and rainfall). This can also give useful information on performances of cropping systems under the different management practices. The actual yield deviation from the different cropping systems and water management interactions were compared based on Eq. [(1):](#page-4-0)

$$dYa = \left[\frac{Yx - Ya}{Ya}\right] \times 100\tag{1}$$

# *2.7. Cropping systems and water management options for agroecological intensification*

Sole (WT, BA, and CP) and rotation (WT-BA and WT-BA-CP) longterm cropping system average yield per case study site under rainfed, tied ridging and four irrigation scenarios were compared and evaluated. Role of integration of legume in cereal dominated cropping systems and soil and water management practices (tied ridging/irrigation) in enhancing agroecosystem intensification was discussed for addressing existing challenges smallholder farmers. For the long-term crop rotation system with legume (WT-BA-CP), nitrogen fixed and uptake by chickpea as well as the average loss of nitrogen through leaching was simulated and tabulated for both study sites (Adigudom and Kulumsa). Crop water productivity was calculated to evaluate the productivity of cropping systems under the different soil water management scenarios in both Adigudom and Kulumsa locations. Crop water productivity (CWP) was calculated as simulated yield (kg/ha) divided by the seasonal crop

evapotranspiration (mm).

## *3.3. Model calibration and validation*

Model calibration dataset under tied ridging and control treatments during the cropping season in 2010 at Mekelle and Maychew sites is presented in Table S2. The model was calibrated for days to flowering and maturity with a percent of deviation of 0–2.3 %. Similarly, the model predicted the measured biomass with normalized root mean square of error (NRMSE) of 14.5 %, index of agreement (I) of 0.99 and R2 of 0.5. The experimental barley yield was simulated by the model with I of 0.7, R2 of 0.5 and NRME of 15.9 %. For the validation dataset, the model was validated for biomass with an I, R2 and NRMSE of 0.96, 08 and 4.8 %, respectively. The corresponding value for yield were 0.96, 0.5 and 13 %, respectively. In addition, the days to flowering and maturity were simulated with percent of deviation of 6.7 and 1.1 %, respectively. Overall, the statistical goodness of fit test result showed that the model was calibrated satisfactorily.

## **4. Discussion**

## *4.1. Modeling soil water in relation to tied ridging and rainfall*

Tied ridging increased soil water throughout the growing season when compared to the control. We found out that the implementation of tied ridging increase the soil water at least by 23 – 27 mm over the soil profile. Other reports also showed that tied ridging reduces runoff and improves rain infiltration and soil water availability, which could be one of the most important strategies for reducing impacts of dry spells in semi-arid areas ([Biazin and Stroosnijder, 2012;](#page-10-0) [Silungwe et al., 2018](#page-11-0)). Araya and Stroonijder (2010) obtained relatively higher (16–30%) and lower (6%) runoff values in field experiment without and with tied ridging, respectively. The authors reported tied ringing improved soil water by up to 27 % in the soil profile. In some of the seasons, the soil water stored was poorly related to the rainfall received during a given period. For example, the measured 10-daily profile soil water was moderately related (R2 = 0.5 to 0.76) to the 10-daily rainfall sum in some of the seasons while not in some of the seasons (R2 = 0). This implied soil water availability might be affected by many factors other than tied ridging (soil water conservation) such as rainfall intensity, slope, soil structure, texture, soil cover, etc.

## *4.2. Impacts of water management practices*

Moderate (5–17 %) yield increase was observed for barley under tied ridging simulation compared to the corresponding water limited yield (Yw). This was because of the cultivars selected for this simulation study are short duration, early maturing and drought tolerant. In most cases, the seasonal rainfall coincides with the growth duration of the cultivar. Thus, the small yield increase may not be significant enough to compensate the cost involved for making the ridges and ties. [Araya and](#page-10-0)  [Stroosnijder (2010)](#page-10-0) reported that tied ridging is only useful under short and erratic rainfall season, particularly, for rainfall ending in less than 60 days regardless of the amount or the rainfall received. In southern Ethiopia, tied ridging was recommended for maize in low rainfall seasons between 280–333 mm ([Biazin and Stroosnijder, 2012](#page-10-0)). Tied ridging improved wheat yield (7–20 %) relative to the water-limited yield depending on the growing season at Adigudom. However, it was found to be less efficient (3–6 %) at Kulumsa site due to the better rainfall distribution in most of the crop growing seasons as compared to Adigudom site. Tied ridging can be considered as a rainwater conservation option for wheat in semi-arid regions where seasonal rainfall hardly meets crop water needs as in case of Adigudom. According to [Silungwe](#page-11-0) [et al. (2018)](#page-11-0), tied ridging is the most suitable practice under smallholder management for reducing soil water stress in Africa. Tied ridging lengthens availability of soil water during below average rainfall years resulting in relatively higher yield compared to those with conventional

practices ([Wiyo et al., 2000;](#page-11-0) [Araya and Stroosnjider, 2020;](#page-10-0) [Biazin and](#page-10-0)  [Stroosnjider, 2012)](#page-10-0). In-situ water harvesting reduces runoff and erosion, increases infiltration, rainwater use efficiency, nutrient status and prolong the presence of soil water for better crop growth ([Araya and](#page-10-0)  [Stroosnjider, 2010;](#page-10-0) [Nyamangara and Nyagumbo, 2010;](#page-10-0) [Miriti et al.,](#page-10-0)  [2012;](#page-10-0) [Nyamadzawo et al., 2013;](#page-10-0) [Silungwe et al., 2018)](#page-11-0). [Wiyo et al.](#page-11-0)  [(2000)](#page-11-0) reported tied ridging as suitable technology for maize in Malawi especially if implemented in fine textured but not in coarse textured soils.

There was also significant increase in chickpea yield due to tied ridging and irrigation. Chickpea yield can go up to 5 Mg/ha with the best agronomic management [(Korbu et al., 2016](#page-10-0)). For example, in our study chickpea yield under irrigation (Yi4) increased substantially (*>*110 %) when compared to the corresponding Ya at both sites. There was considerable yield increase at least by more than 132 % in chickpea yield under treatment simulating tied ridging practice relative to the Ya. As chickpea is planted late in the season, it is usually affected by moisture stress ([Telaye et al., 1994](#page-11-0); [Korbu et al., 2016](#page-10-0)). Therefore, based on this simulation study, the practice of early planting along with the implementation of tied ridging practices and irrigation could substantially improve chickpea yield. In Semi-arid Zimbabwe, [Nyamangara and](#page-10-0)  [Nyagumbo (2010)](#page-10-0) reported the yield enhancing impacts of tied ridging on crops further enhanced when soil fertility management strategies are implemented. Large-scale implementations of water harvesting can help enhance storage of soil water, reduce drought risk, improve yield, increase water productivity and improve resilience and have positive impact on agroecosystem [(Rockstrom et al., 2002;](#page-10-0) [Dile et al., 2013)](#page-10-0). Similarly, avoiding water stress during the reproductive stages of crop development which are highly sensitive to stress can help maintain or improve crop yield of major cereal and legumes crops ([Prasad et al.,](#page-10-0) [2008;](#page-10-0) [Pradhan et al., 2012](#page-10-0); [Sehgal et al., 2018)](#page-10-0).

#### *4.3. Impacts of cropping systems*

One component of agroecological intensification strategy is inclusion of legumes in cropping system. In this study, the inclusion of chickpea in cropping system was evaluated. Chickpea yield increased by more than 50 % with four irrigations. In cropping system including chickpea in rotations (WT-BA-CP) did not significantly increase in its productivity compared to corresponding aggregated yield in WT-BA. This may be due to availability of nitrogen fertilizer but this may not be the case when adequate nitrogen supply is limited. For example, some studies reported a significant increase in cereal yield when rotated with legumes (cowpea; *Vigna unguiculata* L.) compared to continuous cropping ([Kouyate et al., 2000)](#page-10-0). The water-limited yield was increased by maximum of 59 % in the rotation WT-BA relative to Ya while it was increased by maximum of 43 % in rotation WT-BA-CH. However, with tied ridge, the yield in rotation WT-BA-CH and WT-BA increased by 23–40 and 13–17 %, respectively, relative to the Yw. Therefore, the rotation WT-BA-CH was more suitable with tied ridging or irrigation compared to that of rotation WT-BA. Studies showed that integration of legumes in cereal dominated cropping system with soil and water conservation and irrigation practices could improve productivity ([Tadesse](#page-11-0)  [et al., 2015](#page-11-0); [Gebremariam, 2015](#page-10-0)). Better performance of crop rotations compared to sole cropping ([Alhameid et al., 2017)](#page-9-0) was probably due to improved crop resilience to pests and diseases under rotation as compared to mono-cropping ([Alhameid et al., 2017;](#page-9-0) [Arriaga et al., 2017;](#page-10-0) [Farina et al., 2018](#page-10-0)). Although our modeling did not include the impacts on biotic factors. Crop rotation systems increase crop diversity, reduce pests and disease pressure and suppress weeds, which makes it as low input cropping system ([Vanlauwe et al., 2019)](#page-11-0). Crop rotation could reduce cost of pesticide application ([Arriaga et al., 2017)](#page-10-0).

Crop water productivity of the rotation system in WT-BA-CH was relatively higher under irrigated or tied ridging condition than rainfed. This was because chickpea generated substantially higher crop water productivity with soil water management practices than growing under rainfed condition. This study showed that the rotation WT-BA-CH could potentially improve CWP if grown with tied ridging especially during the chickpea-growing season.

This simulation study indicated that irrigation or availability of adequate soil water might play to enhance fixing of N. Chickpea grown at Adigudom site under dryland condition added gross N of 90 kg N/ha on sand clay loam and clay soils. Whereas, chickpea grown at the same site under irrigated (with tied ridging) condition added gross N of up to 167 kg/ha. One of the reasons why fixing of N was relatively lower under dryland than irrigated condition was because fixation is affected by nodule mass and temperature, while nodule growth is affected by temperature and water ([Boote et al., 2008](#page-10-0)). High soil temperature decreased nodule numbers and nodule dry weights in peanut (*Arachis hypogaea* L.; [Prasad et al., 2001)](#page-10-0). Other studies indicated that legumes improve soil nutrient availability and water retention ([Stagnari et al.,](#page-11-0)  [2017)](#page-11-0). In addition to fixing of N by the legume crop, the current study showed that there was substantial residual material remaining in the soil, which could have positive contribution to the soil organic carbon. Rotating crops with legumes have ecological and environmental advantage, with plants exploring different sections of the soil profile permitting access to water and nutrients from various soil depths.

The N uptake rate at one of our site, Kulumsa site, was 87 kg/ha in clay soil. Nitrogen mobilization, leaching and uptake can affect the soil N for next crop (Salmeron ´ [et al., 2014)](#page-10-0). Crop grown after legumes (in rotation) had relatively better performance in terms of crop N utilization (low input system) compared to that continuous cereal. This is because N fertilizer was not applied to chickpea (which could have cost reduction implication) while organic N was added to the soil by chickpea. [Anglade et al. (2015)](#page-10-0) reported that inclusion of legumes is sustainable in terms of both long and short-term crop productivity and N management. Grain legumes fix atmospheric nitrogen, contribute quality organic residue for the next crop as a result significantly improved grain yield of cereals. Although the benefits may vary depending on agroecology, initial soil nutrient conditions, management practices, biophysical and socio-economic conditions ([Ojiem et al., 2006](#page-10-0); [Franke et al., 2017](#page-10-0)). Inclusion of legumes in crop rotation system could add up to 120 kg N/ha ([Grain Research Development Corporation (GRDC), 2013;](#page-10-0) [Korbu](#page-10-0)  [et al., 2016)](#page-10-0) and increase nitrate N in the soil by up to 30− 40 kg/ha and as a result could contribute to improve wheat yield by 0.5–1.5 Mg/ha ([Grain Research Development Corporation (GRDC), 2013](#page-10-0)).

In this study higher leaching loss occurred in sand clay loam compared to clay soils. Studies showed that nitrate N loss are higher in coarser than fine textured soils ([USDA, 2014a](#page-11-0), [2014b;](#page-11-0) [Araya et al.,](#page-10-0)  [2020)](#page-10-0). For example, at Adigudom site, about 45–49 and 21− 27 kg N/ha, leaching was simulated under sand clay loam and clay soils, respectively. Nitrogen leaching loss of 44–98 kg N/ha was estimated depending on soil type (Salmeron ´ [et al., 2014)](#page-10-0). Reports showed that N rate and irrigation determine the fate of N for leaching ([Salmeron](#page-10-0) ´ et al., [2014)](#page-10-0). Grain legumes contribute to soil fertility resulting reduced use of N fertilizer ([Franke et al., 2017)](#page-10-0). For example, crop productivity can increase by up to 50 % for crops following legumes under low input systems ([Stagnari et al., 2017)](#page-11-0). In the current study, although there was minor increase, no significant change in yield was simulated due to presence of legume, may be because near optimal N fertilizer was applied to cereals that follow the legume crop. Growing maize after legume in some African countries increased yield by 0.5 to more than 3 Mg/ha ([Vanlauwe et al., 2019](#page-11-0)). Planting date options could be one advantage. For example, there are planting times that are more suitable for chickpea but not to other crops. For example, when the first crop fails due to abiotic or biotic factors, Ethiopian famers usually replant chickpea late in the season. Crop rotation has also substantial contribution to human nutrition (such as source of cheap protein to resource poor farmers) and health aspects. Chickpea contains protein (20–24 %), carbohydrate (52–71 %), fiber and other minerals (10–23 %), fat (4–10 %) and high in vitamins like B1 and B2 and C ([Korbu et al., 2016)](#page-10-0). It also improves soil health ([Arriaga et al., 2017](#page-10-0)) and soil organic carbon

<span id="page-9-0"></span>([Stagnari et al., 2017](#page-11-0); [Singh et al., 2018)](#page-11-0). These are significant economic resources for poor farmers in semi-arid regions of Africa as biological N fixation is critical for restoring the environment and improving the quality of the soil. Other economic advantages such as crop diversity could improve income due to balanced market supply and demand or market diversity (Francis, 2005; Alhameid et al., 2017). In Malawi, legume-maize rotation is economically more profitable and attractive than continuous maize ([Franke et al., 2014](#page-10-0), [2017](#page-10-0); [Vanlauwe et al.,](#page-11-0)  [2019)](#page-11-0). Apart from economic advantage, in highlands of Africa, growing chickpea in rotation with cereals brought substantial positive effect on maintaining the ecological services ([Vanlauwe et al., 2019)](#page-11-0); reduce emission of greenhouse gasses ([Stagnari et al., 2017)](#page-11-0). Programs where combination of soil fertility management (that include legumes), conservation agriculture practices, soil and water conservation (that include tied ridging) were implemented contributed to agroecological intensification [(Gebremariam, 2015;](#page-10-0) [Balehegn et al., 2019](#page-10-0)).

### *4.4. Limitations of the study and assumptions*

Only two sites are selected as case study for evaluating impacts of cropping systems and water management on yield performance of selected dominant cropping systems in highlands of Africa. Main rainy season especially in northern Ethiopia often ceases in early September, which is approximately at the time of heading to flowering period for the main food crops like barley and wheat, which often suffer from water stress. Cropped fields with tied ridging would increase their resilience to drought stress due to improved infiltration, reduced runoff and increased soil water storage. In this study, we have included mid to late season irrigation as a scenario although majority of producers grow cereals under rainfed condition. However, there are some places where irrigation is practiced even though the size is small from country level. Thus, the inclusion of four irrigation as a scenario might guide producers in identifying cropping system and water management strategies relevant to irrigation water use. The limitation of this study is that the profile soil water might vary by soil type (depth, texture, drainage characteristics, slope, rainfall intensity, crop cover, initial soil water conditions etc.). The work conducted in one place may not perfectly fit to another location (due to climate, soil, cover, slope variations). The collected profile soil water data is approximately every 10-days for up to 0.6 m (with in the effective rooting zone) not from deep soil. Another limitation of this study is that the work did not include economic feasibility of growing legumes (such as chickpea) and implementation of soil and water management practices (such as tied ridging) due to data limitation. In the future, the economic viability of growing legumes (chickpea) in cereal system and soil water management practices need to be studies.

Additional limitation of this study is that tied ridge was not tested for wheat and chickpea in our field experimentations. However, the tied ridging simulated in barley is assumed to work for wheat and chickpea because tied ridging has been reported to increase soil water and reduce runoff for improving yield of various crop as reported for many crops ([Biazin and Stroosnijder, 2012;](#page-10-0) [Miriti et al., 2012;](#page-10-0) [Okeyo et al., 2014](#page-10-0); [Kiboi et al., 2017](#page-10-0); [Silungwe et al., 2018](#page-11-0)). Therefore, although the experimental data is limited to barley, the presented information on the impacts of tied ridging on reducing runoff, enhancing infiltration and soil water storage and increasing yield is consistent with other reviewed materials in similar semi-arid region on other crops [(Araya and Stroos](#page-10-0)[nijder, 2010;](#page-10-0) [Biazin and Stroosnijder, 2012;](#page-10-0) [Miriti et al., 2012](#page-10-0); [Okeyo](#page-10-0)  [et al., 2014;](#page-10-0) [Kiboi et al., 2017;](#page-10-0) [Silungwe et al., 2018](#page-11-0)).

## **5. Conclusions**

Water is one of the limiting factors contributing to lower yields at both locations although the severity of stress differs by crop, growing season and soil types. Of all crops in the continuous and rotation system, wheat and barley were less to moderately affected by water stress

because the modeled cultivar were short maturing and drought tolerant. In contrast, chickpea was strongly affected by water stress. The large yield gap for chickpea at both sites was attributed mainly to water stress. Simulating tied ridging and irrigation significantly improved chickpea yield at both Adigudom and Kulumsa sites and can have yield benefits during dry years. Yields from continuous cropping (monoculture) and crop rotations were also substantially affected by water stress. Average long-term aggregated yield for the rotations WT-BA-CP at Kulumsa site was substantially greater than that of Adigudom site, which implies relatively better crop growing conditions at Kulumsa. Chickpea fixed substantial amount of organic nitrogen (90–246 kg N/ha) in the soil, which could have yield benefits, cost reduction and environmental benefit due to added organic nitrogen and reduced inorganic nitrogen use. Overall, the WT-BA-CP rotation was relatively more responsive to irrigation/tied ridging practice compared to the WT-BA rotation. In addition, there could be other benefits of crop rotation systems that include managing pests and diseases and nutrient recycling. The model was not calibrated to simulate pest/disease due to lack of data. However, the results of this modeling case study and evidence from literature across Africa highlights the significance of integrating diverse cropping systems (that include legumes) and water management practices (tied ridges and irrigation) for agroecological intensification.
