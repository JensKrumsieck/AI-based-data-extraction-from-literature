# Integrated management of irrigation water and fertilizers for wheat crop using field experiments and simulation modeling

## S.K. Behera, R.K. Panda *

Agricultural and Food Engineering Department, Indian Institute of Technology, Kharagpur (WB) 721 302, India

#### ARTICLE INFO

#### ABSTRACT

Article history: Received 15 June 2008 Accepted 14 June 2009 Available online 19 July 2009

Keywords: Irrigation schedule Fertilization Water use efficiency Depletion level CERES-wheat

The reported study aimed at developing an integrated management strategy for irrigation water and fertilizers in case of wheat crop in a sub-tropical sub-humid region. Field experiments were conducted on wheat crop (cultivar Sonalika) during the years 2002–2003, 2003–2004 and 2004–2005. Each experiment included four fertilizer treatments and three irrigation treatments during the wheat growth period. During the experiment, the irrigation treatments considered were I1 = 10% maximum allowable depletion (MAD) of available soil water (ASW); I2 = 40% MAD of ASW; I3 = 60% MAD of ASW. The fertilizer treatments considered in the experiments were F1 = control treatment with N:P2O5:K2O as 0:0:0 kg ha-1 , F2 = fertilizer application of N:P2O5:K2O as 80:40:40 kg ha-1 ; F3 = fertilizer application of N:P2O5:K2O as 120:60:60 kg ha-1 and F4 = fertilizer application of N:P2O5:K2O as 160:80:80 kg ha-1 . In this study CERES-wheat crop growth model of the DSSAT v4.0 was used to simulate the growth, development and yield of wheat crop using soil, daily weather and management inputs, to aid farmers and decision makers in developing strategies for effective management of inputs. The results of the investigation revealed that magnitudes of grain yield, straw yield and maximum LAI of wheat crop were higher in low volume high frequency irrigation (I1) than the high volume low frequency irrigation (I3). The grain yield, straw yield and maximum LAI increased with increase in fertilization rate for the wheat crop. The results also revealed that increase in level of fertilization increased water use efficiency (WUE) considerably. However, WUE of the I2 irrigation schedule was comparatively higher than the I1 and I3 irrigation schedules due to higher grain yield per unit use of water. Therefore, irrigation schedule with 40% maximum allowable depletion of available soil water (I2) could safely be maintained during the noncritical stages to save water without sacrificing the crop yield. Increase in level of fertilization increases the WUE but it will cause environmental problem beyond certain limit. The calibrated CERES-wheat model could predict the grain yield, straw yield and maximum LAI of wheat crop with considerable accuracy and therefore can be recommended for decision-making in similar regions.

#### 1. Introduction

The ever-increasing target of food grain production requires intensive use of chemical fertilizer and water in agriculture, which leads to both higher production cost and greater risk of environmental pollution. In twenty first century the major targets of global agriculture are to attain food security and environmental stability. Wheat is one of the most important and popular major crops all over the world. Wheat is grown in an area of about 19–20 million hectares in India and nearly 54% of this area is irrigated. That constitutes about 27% of the irrigated area of the country. Well-drained clay loams, loams and sandy loams are suitable for this crop. To increase the agricultural production, there has been a tendency to adopt high application rate of fertilizer and irrigation water, often together ([Hussain and AI-Jaloud, 1995](#page-7-0)). Besides high cost of production, degradation of water and soil quality owing to overuse of agrochemicals is a matter of global concern. Therefore, water and fertilizer inputs should be carefully managed to avoid losses. Higher water use efficiency is attained through new irrigation technology by reducing percolation and runoff losses [(Li](#page-8-0) [et al., 2004; Garabet et al., 1998](#page-8-0)).

Increase in rate of fertilizer application increases the grain and straw yield of different wheat varieties. The losses of fertilizer however increase at higher rates of fertilizer N application ([Timsina et al., 2001](#page-8-0)). Dry matter production of crop is a function of fertilizer use. Fertilization results in an increase in leaf area and photosynthesis, which in turn influence dry matter accumulation. Nitrogen fertilization also increases cell size, elongation and division that influence growth parameters like plant height ([Sharma et al., 1990](#page-8-0)). Yield response to fertilizer nitrogen has been reported to be different in different agroclimatic regions of India. There is a close relation between amount of applied N to soil,

Therefore, the challenge for farmers is to convert the applied N in soil to grain yield with maximum efficacy because N is one of the most costly inputs to rice–wheat system ([Latiri-souki et al., 1998; Wilkinson et al.,](#page-8-0) [2000](#page-8-0)). From a series of field experiments [(Garabet et al., 1998; Li](#page-7-0) [et al., 2004)](#page-7-0), it was revealed that increasing the dose of nitrogen beyond 120 kg N ha-1 did not add significantly to the yield of wheat.

Irrigation is one of the important inputs controlling the productivity of wheat crop. However, improper application of irrigation water may be detrimental to the productivity of the crop ([Zwart and Bastiaanssen, 2004; Sarkar, 2004](#page-8-0)). In eastern India, one of the reasons for the decline in yield of wheat crop in recent years is improper management of irrigation water. Proper management of irrigation water is needed for sustainable productivity and increased water use efficiency of wheat crop ([Sarkar, 2004](#page-8-0)). Deficit irrigation during plant growth stages that are relatively insensitive to water stress will increase the water use efficiency ([Zhang et al.,](#page-8-0) [1999)](#page-8-0). [Panda et al. (2003)](#page-8-0) found that under conditions of water scarcity, irrigation can be scheduled at 45% maximum allowable depletion of available soil water for the non-critical stages of growth of wheat crop grown in sandy loam soils in sub-humid subtropical regions in order to obtain maximum grain yield and above ground dry matter as well as maximum field water use efficiency. It was also observed that proper scheduling of irrigation, so as to apply required quantity of water during the moisture sensitive stages of flowering and seed formation, yet allowing moderate stress at vegetative and maturity stages produce the optimum yield with maximum water use efficiency and water economy in most of the crops ([Reddy and Reddy, 1993)](#page-8-0).

Crop simulation models (CSMs) have been used extensively in India to quantify the change in yield potential at different levels of management and climatic variability. It was also proved that the simulation studies can supplement the field studies in decisionmaking ([Aggarwal et al., 1994](#page-7-0)). Selection of most appropriate model for a particular situation depends on its envisaged application and the quality of available input data [(Ma et al.,](#page-8-0) [2002; Ziaei and Sepaskhah, 2003](#page-8-0)). The Decision Support System for Agro-technology Transfer (DSSAT), which is a combination of several dynamic crop simulation models, is one such models that can predict accurately the growth, development and yield of wheat crop with the help of soil, daily weather and management inputs, to aid farmers in developing long term rotational strategies. DSSAT has a unique feature of using historical or forecasted weather data to predict the yields under different management options [(Tsuji](#page-8-0) [et al., 1994; Jagtap et al., 2003; Stastana et al., 2002](#page-8-0)).

With these backgrounds, a comprehensive field investigation was undertaken on a coarse textured acid lateritic soil (Alfisol) at the experimental farm of the Indian Institute of Technology, Kharagpur. The study was an attempt to assess the effect of fertilization and irrigation schedule on crop growth, yield parameters and water use efficiency of wheat crop. The specific objectives of the study were (1) to examine the integrated effect of fertilization levels and irrigation schedule on yield and water use efficiency of wheat through field study, which may help in better management of irrigation water and fertilizer for wheat under subhumid sub-tropical condition; (2) to test the performance of CERES-wheat in simulating growth and yield of wheat crop under different levels of fertilization and irrigation schedule.

The experimental area was divided into 36 plots of 5 m 4 m size maintaining a buffer of 1 m between adjacent plots. ### 2.3. Irrigation scheduling

Irrigation scheduling was based on the percentage depletion of available soil water in the root zone. Available soil water was considered as the difference between root zone water storage at field capacity and permanent wilting point. For estimating available soil water storage, the irrigation management depth of the wheat crop was assumed as 60 cm, irrespective of the growth stage ([Panda et al., 2003)](#page-8-0). The maximum allowable depletions (MAD) of the available soil water were fixed at 10, 40 and 60%. In order to assess the changes in soil water status, soil moisture was measured in 0–15, 15–30, 30–45, 45–60, and 60– 90 cm layers of soil profile. In top 0–15 cm layer where the neutron probe (Hydroprobe) could not be used, moisture content was measured gravimetrically. An access tube made of seamless aluminum alloy (50 cm internal diameter) was installed in the center of each experimental plot for soil moisture measurements using the neutron probe. The time changes of soil moisture at 15– 30, 30–45, 45–60, and 60–90 cm soil layers were estimated with the help of Hydroprobe (Model 503 of Campbell Pacific Nuclear Corporation, U.S.A.). Calibration curves showing the relationship between the neutron probe count ratio and volumetric soil water content were prepared for the experimental soil profile prior to the start of the experiments. Using the temporal data of soil moisture measured by a neutron probe and gravimetric measurements, the percentage depletion of available soil water in the effective root zone was estimated by the following equation ([Martin et al., 1990](#page-8-0)):

maximum allowable depletion ð%Þ

$$= 100 \times \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i}^{n} \frac{F\mathbf{C}_{i} - \theta_{i}}{F\mathbf{C}_{i} - \text{PWP}} \tag{2.1}$$

where n is the number of layers in the effective rooting depth used for the soil moisture sampling, FCi is the soil moisture at field capacity in the ith layer, ui is the soil moisture in ith layer before irrigation and PWP is the soil moisture at permanent wilting point. The amount of water applied after the attainment of predefined MAD was calculated as

$$V_d = \frac{\mathbf{MAD}(\%) \times (\mathbf{FC} - \mathbf{PWP}) \times \mathbf{R}_{\mathbb{Z}} \times \mathbf{A}}{100} \tag{2.2}$$

where Vd is the volume of irrigation water (m3 ), Rz is the effective rooting depth (m) and A is the surface area of the plot (m2 ). The surface area of each plot was 20 m2 while the effective root zone depth was assumed as 60 cm for all treatments. Measured volume of irrigation water was applied to each plot with the help of a hosepipe and a water meter. The water meter was calibrated and tested for accuracy before its use. Water was introduced at one end and was allowed to rapidly spread over the entire basin. Equal amount of irrigation was applied for each treatment up to 30 days after sowing. Different irrigation treatments were maintained 30 days after sowing by applying different volumes of irrigation water at different intervals under different irrigation regimes. For example, in case of I1 irrigation treatment, 10 numbers of irrigations amounting to 12 mm of water was applied at 4–5 days interval. In case of I2 irrigation treatment, 2 numbers of irrigations amounting to 48 mm was applied at 20–25 days interval whereas in case of I3 irrigation treatment, single irrigation amounting 72 mm was applied at 30 days interval during all the crop experiments.

#### 2.4. Growth parameters

In order to evaluate the effect of different levels of fertilizer application and moisture regimes on crop responses, data on the growth attributes of wheat crop were collected. Postharvest data pertaining to the straw yield and grain yield were measured on the day before general harvest of wheat crop. These data were necessary for estimation of water use efficiency and for evaluating the effects of different treatments on the final crop yield. The growth parameters, such as plant height, number of branches and leaves, and leaf area index (LAI) were recorded at an interval of 15 days after the establishment of the crop. Two representative plants were harvested from each treatment plot for determination of the above growth parameters. The maximum leaf area index (LAI) was calculated from the LAI data collected at different growth stages.

#### 2.5. Crop water use (WU) and water use efficiency (WUE)

Crop water used (WU) by the crop was estimated from the depletion of soil water storage in the root zone taking into account rainfall, irrigation and deep percolation, and computing the water balance as [(Sahoo, 2004)](#page-8-0):

$$\mathbf{W}\mathbf{U} = \sum_{t_1}^{t_2} \Delta \mathbf{s} \Delta \mathbf{t} + (\mathbf{R} + I) - DP \tag{2.3}$$

where WU is the crop water use in cm, Ds is the change in soil water storage in the root zone in cm, R is the rainfall in cm, I is irrigation applied in cm, DP is the deep percolation loss in cm and t1 and t2 are the start and end of the time interval under consideration.

Water use efficiency (WUE) was estimated as

$$\text{WUE} = \frac{Y}{\text{WU}} \text{ kg ha}^{-1} \text{cm}^{-1} \tag{2.4}$$

where Y was grain yield in kg ha-1 .

#### 2.6. Simulation of crop growth

DSSAT (Decision Support System for Agrotechnology Transfer) was used for simulating crop growth parameters ([Hoogenboom](#page-7-0) [et al., 1994](#page-7-0)). DSSAT is an integrated software of different computer programmes, which facilitate the application of crop simulation models in research and decision-making. For the present study CERES-wheat model from DSSAT v4.0 was used for the wheat crop. In CERES model, the input and output files carry data related to soil, weather, genotype and crop. The files structure is described in three different categories: input files, output files and experimental data files. Input files are further divided into those dealing with the experiment, weather and soil, and the characteristics of different genotypes (crop and cultivar).

#### 2.6.1. Experimental detail file

This input file structure has maximum flexibility but it requires minimum data set to run the simulation. The structure is designed to vary input for a variety of crop, soil, fertilizer level, and weather for same experimental file. The file heading contains the experiment code and name, the treatment combinations, and details of the experimental conditions (field characteristics, soil analysis data, initial soil water and inorganic nitrogen conditions, seedbed preparation and planting geometries, irrigation and water management, fertilizer management, organic residue applications, chemical applications, tillage operations, environmental modifications, harvest management), and simulation controls. In experimental input file, there is a separate simulation section, which was used to simulate and run the crop model under the situation already mentioned in input files and to get the output results in the form of growth and yield of crops.

#### 2.6.2. Weather data file

Daily weather data at least from planting date to harvest date was loaded for each crop growth period separately to run a particular crop file. The minimum weather data, such as daily solar radiation, maximum, minimum temperature and rainfall were included. This minimum data set of weather parameters was used in the calibration and validation of CERES-wheat. Solar radiation was estimated from bright sunshine hours using the standard Angstrom formula [(Angstrom, 1924](#page-7-0)). The other required informations, such as name of the country, latitude, longitude, elevation, etc. were also used to create the weather data file for the station of Indian Institute of Technology, Kharagpur.

#### 2.6.3. Soil data file

The data related to soil profile, soil water, soil nitrogen and root growth characteristics, soil taxonomic classification, soil texture and other descriptive data of the experimental site were used to develop the soil file for the experimental station. The file generally contains information that is available for the soil at a particular experimental site, and supplementary information extracted from a soil survey database for a soil of the same taxonomic classification as the soil at the experimental site. The data in the soils file are arranged so that entries need to be made only for the aspects simulated.

#### 2.6.4. Genotype

The data related to morphological and physiological characteristics of a crop are used to create the genotype input file. This file was used by the model to create several genetic coefficients, which described how the genotype of a specific crop variety was influenced by the environmental factors, such as maximum and minimum temperatures, day length, etc. Genetic coefficients of CERES-wheat are related to photoperiod sensitivity, duration of grain filling, conversion of mass to grain number, grain filling rates, vernalization requirement, stem size and cold hardiness.

#### 3. Results and discussion

#### 3.1. Growth and yield responses of wheat

Wheat crop was grown during three consecutive years, such as 2002–2003, 2003–2004 and 2004–2005. #### 4. Conclusions

The response ofwheat crop as influenced by irrigation scheduling and levels of fertilization was characterized by grain yield, straw yield and maximum leaf area index (LAI). No significant difference in grain yield, straw yield and maximum LAI is observed in case of I1 and I2 irrigation treatments, whereas significant difference in grain yield, straw yield andmaximum LAI is found in case of I2 and I3due to delayed irrigation. With increase in MAD to 60% (I3), the yield reduces drastically due to significant reduction in the availability of water and fertilizer. However, WUE of the I2 irrigation schedule is noted to be comparatively higher than the I1 and I3 irrigation schedules due to higher grain yield per unit use of water. Therefore, under water scarcity condition, irrigation schedule with 40% maximum allowable depletion of available soil water could safely be maintained during the non-critical stages of crop growth to save waterwithout sacrificing the crop yield in order to obtain the highest water use efficiency (WUE) for wheat crop in sandy loam soil. The simulated results reveals that CERES-wheat predicts the effect of different levels of fertilization and irrigation schedules on grain yield, straw yield and maximum LAI of wheat crop quite well. Therefore, the CERES-wheat model can successfully be used for simulation of grain yield, straw yield and maximum LAI of wheat crop in sub-humid sub-tropical regions.
